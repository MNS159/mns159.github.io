Trump drops Strait of Hormuz toll plan after Gulf states pledge US investments

President Trump abandoned his proposed 20% fee on ships transiting the Strait of Hormuz after Gulf leaders offered alternative US investments. The reversal came as the US prepares to reinstate a naval blockade on Iranian ports at 4 p.m. ET, escalating tensions amid ongoing attacks on commercial shipping.

The proposed toll faced immediate backlash from the International Maritime Organization, which called it illegal under international law. Major shipping companies like Hapag-Lloyd also rejected the concept of paying for passage through international waters, creating early pressure for the policy reversal.

Trump's initial announcement marked a stark departure from longstanding US opposition to strait tolls. Just last month, Secretary of State Marco Rubio had affirmed that 'no country is allowed to charge tolls or fees on an international waterway' under existing maritime law.

The investment deals Trump referenced remain undefined, raising questions about whether they represent new commitments or repackaged agreements from his 2025 Middle East visit. Gulf states including Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Qatar reportedly proposed the alternative arrangement during calls with the president.

Oil markets reacted swiftly to the developments, with Brent crude initially spiking above $85 before retreating slightly after the toll cancellation. However, prices remain elevated due to the impending Iranian port blockade and continued attacks on tankers, including ADNOC vessels that suffered casualties this week.

The US Navy's blockade reinstatement at 4 p.m. ET will test the fragile interim agreement with Iran, which had pledged 60 days of toll-free passage. Analysts warn the dual pressures of blockade and ongoing military strikes could collapse the temporary deal, prolonging oil market disruptions.

US reimposes Iran naval blockade as ceasefire collapses, spiking oil prices

The U.S. will reinstate a naval blockade on Iranian ports starting Tuesday evening ET, reversing a brief ceasefire and triggering a 4.8% surge in global oil prices. The move follows Iran's attacks on shipping in the Strait of Hormuz, where 20% of traded oil transits, and marks a return to April's conflict levels.

The blockade's resumption reactivates a key pressure tactic from April-June, when U.S. warships intercepted vessels entering/leaving Iranian ports. CENTCOM's 2000 GMT deadline gives commercial shippers minimal time to adjust routes, unlike the phased implementation last spring.

Iran's weekend closure of the strait and subsequent attacks on two ships—including one fatal strike verified by the UAE—directly challenged Trump's abandoned plan to charge 20% tolls. Tehran mocked the proposal as piracy, with Foreign Minister Araghchi offering competing 'discount' protection fees.

Brent crude's jump to $87/barrel reflects renewed fears about the strait's stability, which handles 21 million barrels daily. The April blockade had previously pushed prices above $90, with analysts warning current disruptions could exceed that threshold within days.

Brazil's President Lula condemned the toll proposal as illegal under UNCLOS maritime law, which designates straits as international waterways. The U.S. had previously criticized Iran's own 2019 threats to charge transit fees on the same legal grounds.

The collapsed interim deal had offered sanctions relief for Iranian oil exports in exchange for nuclear program limits. Its failure leaves diplomacy in disarray, with both sides now escalating military actions rather than returning to negotiations.

Supreme Court justices request increased security funding due to rising threats

Supreme Court Justices Amy Coney Barrett and Elena Kagan testified before Congress about the court's 2027 budget request, highlighting a rise in threats against justices. The court is seeking $228.4 million, a 10% increase over 2026, primarily for enhanced security and cybersecurity measures. Barrett shared personal experiences, including a swatting incident and her child seeing a bulletproof vest, while Kagan noted a 38% annual increase in threats to the court.

Implications

The increased funding request reflects growing security concerns for federal judges, including cybersecurity threats exacerbated by advancements in AI. A shift from Marshals Service to Supreme Court Police for residential security indicates a long-term strategy for protection. The testimony underscores the broader trend of rising threats against public officials, which could lead to expanded security protocols across the judiciary.

How different sides see it

Democrats: Democrats emphasize the need for robust security measures to protect justices, aligning with Kagan's call for increased funding. They argue that judicial independence relies on ensuring justices can perform their duties without fear, as highlighted by recent threats and incidents.

Republicans: Republicans support the funding increase, with Rep. Dave Joyce stating that justices must work without fear for their safety. They frame the request as a non-partisan necessity, given the documented rise in threats and the need to modernize cybersecurity defenses.

Apple explores on-device AI through startup partnerships

Apple is in talks with PrismML, a startup that compresses AI models to run directly on iPhones, potentially enabling more complex features like computational photography and health tools without cloud reliance. This move could enhance privacy, reduce latency, and lower cloud costs for Apple's AI strategy. PrismML claims its models use significantly less memory and energy while maintaining performance.

PrismML's technology stems from Caltech research, leveraging simplified data storage methods that reduce AI model sizes from 54 GB to under 4 GB. This efficiency allows models like Alibaba's Qwen to run on iPhones, MacBooks, and PCs. While the compression sacrifices some performance, particularly in factual recall, it enhances speed and energy efficiency, making it viable for on-device applications.

Apple's focus on local AI processing aligns with its broader strategy to make Siri more competitive while safeguarding user privacy. By keeping sensitive data on-device, Apple avoids the privacy risks associated with cloud-based AI systems. This approach could also reduce reliance on third-party cloud providers, lowering operational costs.

The implications of PrismML's technology extend beyond smartphones. Its potential applications include robotics and autonomous systems, where quick, localized decision-making is critical. However, questions remain about how these models will perform under real-world conditions, particularly regarding battery life and reliability across millions of devices.

The startup's advancements could also impact the semiconductor industry. Efficient AI models may reduce demand for high-performance memory chips and datacenter infrastructure, shifting focus toward optimizing existing hardware for AI tasks. PrismML plans to compress larger models, including Google's Gemma, which could further democratize AI accessibility on consumer devices.

The $1.5B target would fund computing infrastructure and talent acquisition ahead of an IPO that could come as early as late 2026. Previous backers include Tencent and Beijing's state-affiliated AI fund, signaling strong institutional support.

New York becomes first state to ban new data center construction

New York Gov. Kathy Hochul signed an executive order halting permits for data centers 50 megawatts or larger, citing environmental and community impact concerns. The moratorium comes as AI-driven computing demands push planned data centers to unprecedented scales, with nearly a quarter projected to exceed 500 megawatts by 2030. The move sets up potential conflict with federal energy policies that have prioritized rapid data center expansion.

The ban reflects growing public skepticism about AI's societal impact, with only 10% of Americans expressing more excitement than concern about AI in daily life according to Pew Research. Hochul's order specifically links data center growth to rising utility bills, water scarcity, and noise pollution—issues that have fueled local opposition nationwide.

New York's action goes beyond temporary measures being considered elsewhere, including a proposed three-year moratorium in the state legislature. Unlike Maine's failed attempt to pause construction, Hochul's order takes immediate effect while establishing a framework for future environmental reviews and potential grid-support fees.

The policy shift marks a dramatic reversal from just years ago, when states competed aggressively for data center projects. Recent surveys show two-thirds of Americans now worry about data centers driving up electricity costs, with preferences shifting toward other industrial developments like warehouses.

The ban directly challenges federal energy priorities under the Trump administration, which recently directed grid operators to create 'fast lanes' for data center interconnections. This conflict may escalate as AI compute demands grow, with BloombergNEF projecting hyperscale facilities will dominate new construction.

Meta sued for allegedly using AI to target employees on medical leave in layoffs

Twenty-six former Meta employees filed a lawsuit alleging the company used AI tools that disproportionately targeted workers with disabilities or on medical leave during recent layoffs. The plaintiffs claim Meta's system ranked employees based on productivity metrics without accounting for protected leaves, violating anti-discrimination laws. Meta denies the claims, stating layoff decisions were made by humans.

The lawsuit centers on Meta's alleged use of multiple AI systems including 'Metamate' and employee productivity dashboards that tracked metrics like AI token usage. These tools reportedly created composite scores used to select employees for termination during May's layoffs of 8,000 workers (10% of staff).

Legal experts note this case could test the boundaries of the Americans with Disabilities Act in the AI era. The plaintiffs argue Meta's system effectively penalized workers for taking legally protected leaves by counting their absence against productivity metrics.

Meta's $115-135B AI investment plans for 2026 may have driven the layoffs, with the company simultaneously reallocating 7,000 employees to AI projects. This restructuring suggests the alleged AI-driven layoffs occurred during a broader pivot toward artificial intelligence development.

The case highlights growing concerns about algorithmic bias in HR decisions. Unlike human managers who might recognize extenuating circumstances, the lawsuit claims Meta's systems automatically downgraded employees who took medical leave without contextual understanding.

If proven, the allegations could set precedent for how companies deploy AI in workforce management. Other tech firms using similar productivity-tracking systems may face scrutiny about whether their algorithms adequately accommodate protected employee statuses.

IBM stock plunges 25% after AI-driven earnings miss and client spending shift

IBM shares crashed 25% — their worst single-day drop since 1987 — after reporting weaker-than-expected Q2 earnings due to clients abruptly shifting spending toward AI infrastructure hardware. The surprise pivot away from IBM's core software and consulting services caught management off guard, with CEO Arvind Krishna admitting the company failed to adapt quickly enough to the market shift.

The earnings miss marks a stark reversal from IBM's strong Q1 performance, where software revenue grew 11% to $7.05 billion. This quarter's $17.2 billion revenue fell $660 million short of estimates, with adjusted EPS of $2.93 missing by $0.08 — gaps Krishna attributed primarily to delayed enterprise software deals as clients reevaluated budgets.

Krishna specifically cited the impact of Anthropic's AI cybersecurity model 'Mythos', which caused enterprises to pause security software purchases while assessing AI integration needs. This reflects broader industry uncertainty as generative AI disrupts traditional software spending patterns, though IBM insists its products aren't directly threatened by AI displacement.

The spending shift toward AI infrastructure benefited memory chip makers like Micron and SK Hynix, whose stocks rose as IBM fell. Clients prioritized securing GPU servers and high-bandwidth memory ahead of expected price hikes, diverting budgets from IBM's higher-margin software and consulting services.

This earnings shock compounds existing investor concerns about IBM's positioning in the AI race. While the company has emphasized its Watsonx AI platform and consulting capabilities, the abrupt capex reallocation suggests enterprises view hardware vendors as more critical to near-term AI implementation.

Congo's Ebola outbreak accelerates with 80% of cases untraceable

The Ebola outbreak in eastern Congo is spreading faster than containment efforts, with 80% of new cases emerging from unknown transmission chains, WHO reports. At least 1,926 infections and 702 deaths have been recorded since May, driven by a rare Bundibugyo strain with no approved treatment. The situation is exacerbated by community deaths, healthcare strikes, and regional conflict.

The Bundibugyo virus variant responsible for this outbreak has a case fatality rate of 25-50%, lower than Zaire Ebola's 60-90%, but its rapid community spread presents unique containment challenges. Unlike previous outbreaks, this strain has no approved vaccine, though phase 1 trials for a CEPI-backed candidate are beginning.

Eastern Congo's armed conflicts have forced 4.5 million people to flee their homes since 2017, creating mobile populations that evade contact tracing. Attacks on 38 health facilities in Ituri Province this year further complicate response efforts.

WHO modeling suggests actual case numbers may be 2-4 times higher than reported, with recent spread to Tshopo and Haut-Uele provinces. Uganda has reported 20 spillover cases, prompting border surveillance reinforcements.

The U.S. CDC confirmed an American aid worker's infection last week, highlighting international risk. Strikes by unpaid health staff at treatment centers compound staffing shortages, with $1.5 billion in pledged international aid facing disbursement delays.
