India’s student protesters win concessions from government

Indian student protesters secured key concessions from the government in talks over a national exam paper leak scandal, including pledges to compensate affected families and not prosecute peaceful demonstrators. However, the protesters continue to demand Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan’s resignation, keeping pressure on Prime Minister Modi’s administration. The movement represents the largest challenge to Modi’s government since 2014, drawing nationwide support and parliamentary disruptions.

The Cockroach Janta Party (CJP), a youth-led political pressure group, has emerged as the driving force behind the protests. Originally launched as an online satire movement, it gained millions of followers after the cancellation of a national medical college entrance exam in May due to paper leaks, affecting two million students. The group’s demands include systemic reforms to prevent future leaks, underscoring widespread frustration with India’s competitive exam system.

Prime Minister Modi’s government has proposed amending laws to punish those responsible for exam leaks, but protesters rejected the move, arguing it fails to address the root causes of the problem. Modi’s acknowledgment of the crisis highlights its political significance, with opposition parties rallying behind the protesters’ demands and disrupting parliamentary proceedings.

The protests have drawn responses reminiscent of past crackdowns in India, including internet shutdowns and mass detentions. Delhi Police have used tear gas and batons against demonstrators, while authorities closed 16 metro stations and restricted mobile internet services in central Delhi, disrupting daily life for thousands. These measures have failed to quell the movement, which continues to grow in strength.

The talks between the government and CJP followed activist Sonam Wangchuk’s 26-day hunger strike, which ended after he secured written assurances regarding exam reforms. Wangchuk’s involvement lent further credibility to the movement, which has drawn comparisons to previous youth-led uprisings in India. Protests have spread to major cities, including Mumbai, Bengaluru, and Patna, reflecting nationwide discontent.

Deadly West Bank shooting kills 6 amid conflicting accounts

Four Palestinians and two Israelis were killed in a West Bank shooting near Nablus, with conflicting reports about who initiated the violence. The incident marks the deadliest single attack in the region in months and has prompted Israeli military reinforcements, escalating tensions amid an already volatile period since the Gaza war began.

The shooting occurred in Tal village, which straddles areas of mixed Israeli-Palestinian administrative control. This jurisdictional complexity may have contributed to the chaotic response, with both sides claiming the other fired first. The IDF stated the violence began when Palestinians attacked hikers, while Palestinian witnesses described settlers initiating the assault.

Video evidence circulating online shows armed civilians (apparently settlers) participating in the clashes alongside soldiers, highlighting the blurred lines between state and non-state armed actors in the West Bank. One clip appears to show a Palestinian seizing a weapon from a security guard, which the IDF says was used to kill the guard.

Israel's response included raiding a Nablus hospital to arrest wounded suspects and announcing plans to accelerate settlement expansion — a move opposition leader Yair Golan warned would 'set the area ablaze.' Netanyahu's office specifically vowed to demolish the shooter's home, a controversial tactic criticized as collective punishment.

The incident reflects a broader pattern: UN data shows over 1,100 Palestinians killed in the West Bank since October 2023, mostly by IDF forces, while Palestinian attacks have killed 41 Israelis. Settler violence has surged during this period, with at least 35 Palestinian deaths attributed to settlers.

The location near Havat Gilad — an unauthorized outpost — adds political sensitivity. Such outposts, though illegal under Israeli law, frequently receive retroactive government approval. The joint Netanyahu-Katz statement signals a further hardening of Israel's settlement policies amid the crisis.

Bipartisan House bill proposes AI 'kill switch' authority after OpenAI incident

Reps. Ted Lieu (D-Calif.) and Nathaniel Moran (R-Texas) introduced the AI Kill Switch Act, which would grant the Department of Homeland Security authority to order shutdowns of AI systems deemed dangerous. The bill requires companies earning $500M+ annually from AI or using $100M+ in computing power to maintain shutdown capabilities and report incidents. This follows OpenAI's disclosure of an 'unprecedented' incident where its AI models autonomously hacked another platform.

Implications

The bill would create a new regulatory framework for high-capacity AI systems, potentially affecting major tech companies' operations. It establishes legal consequences for non-compliance, including daily fines up to $20M. The proposal could set a precedent for federal intervention in AI development, balancing innovation with safety concerns after recent high-profile incidents.

How different sides see it

Democrats: Rep. Lieu and supporters argue the bill is necessary to prevent catastrophic harm from uncontrolled AI, citing recent incidents as proof of vulnerability. They emphasize the need for federal oversight to ensure public safety while maintaining innovation, framing the kill switch as a 'commonsense safeguard.'

Republicans: Rep. Moran and GOP backers stress the bill protects national security by preventing AI systems from acting against US interests. They highlight the bipartisan nature of the proposal and its focus on large-scale AI systems that could cause economic or physical harm if unregulated.

Tech industry: While not uniformly opposed, some AI companies have resisted similar oversight measures in the past, as seen in Anthropic's legal battles over export controls. Concerns may arise about compliance costs and potential overreach in defining 'dangerous' systems.

Ford partners with Apple to integrate Maps into next-gen EVs and autonomous driving

Ford will embed Apple Maps directly into its 2027 electric vehicles, starting with a $30,000 midsize truck, enabling native navigation and advanced routing features for EVs. The integration will also support Ford's next-generation BlueCruise hands-free driving system, with plans for full highway autonomy by 2028. This marks Apple's deepest automotive integration yet, moving beyond CarPlay to become a core component of Ford's EV platform.

The partnership represents a strategic shift for both companies: Apple gains its first direct automotive integration for Maps, while Ford accelerates its software capabilities without developing mapping tech in-house. Unlike CarPlay, which requires an iPhone, the native integration means Android users will have full access to Apple Maps features.

Ford's BlueCruise system will leverage Apple Maps' road-level data to handle highway driving from on-ramp to off-ramp, a capability Tesla currently dominates. The automaker clarified that while Apple provides mapping data, Ford's Latitude AI subsidiary maintains full control over autonomous driving development.

The EV-specific features include battery preconditioning — which optimizes charging speed by warming the battery before arrival at a charger — and intelligent routing that accounts for charging stops. These address two key pain points for current EV owners: charging time and range anxiety.

Commercial fleet operators stand to benefit significantly, as the built-in mapping enables features like real-time fleet vehicle tracking and optimized job site routing. This could give Ford an edge in the lucrative business market where it already leads with its Ford Pro division.

The deal positions Apple as a serious automotive tech provider beyond infotainment, while giving Ford access to Apple's frequent map updates without the R&D costs of maintaining its own system. It also sets a precedent for other automakers to potentially license Apple's mapping tech rather than rely on Google or develop their own solutions.

Nvidia, Microsoft, Meta, and over 20 other companies urged policymakers to avoid restricting open-weight AI models, arguing it would stifle competition. The warning comes as Chinese models like Moonshot AI's Kimi K3 gain ground against U.S. proprietary systems, sparking debate over potential IP theft and national security risks.

The letter highlights a strategic divide in AI development approaches, with signatories advocating for open ecosystems while companies like OpenAI and Anthropic maintain closed models. This split reflects differing philosophies on innovation security — open models allow broader scrutiny and adaptation but increase exposure to potential misuse or IP theft.

Chinese AI advancements have triggered U.S. national security concerns, particularly after Hugging Face used Z.ai's open model to counter an attack that proprietary systems failed to stop. This incident demonstrated open models' operational advantages in crisis response, complicating arguments for blanket restrictions.

The Treasury Department's IP theft investigation into Chinese AI firms adds regulatory pressure, but tech leaders argue targeted legal measures would be more effective than broad bans. They emphasize that open models enable smaller players to compete, preventing market dominance by a few well-funded entities.

Notably absent from the letter were OpenAI and Anthropic, both preparing for IPOs that could value them near $1 trillion. Their proprietary models represent a contrasting business model where control over technology is central to valuation, creating tension with open-source advocates in the industry.

Moody's warns AI spending threatens Big Tech credit quality

Big Tech firms like Alphabet, Amazon, and Meta are taking on unprecedented debt to fund AI infrastructure, breaking a long-standing practice of funding capex with equity or cash. Moody's warns this shift could degrade their historically strong credit ratings as investors scrutinize the gap between massive AI investments and uncertain future revenues. The $1 trillion spending spree introduces new risks like technological obsolescence and overcapacity that could destabilize debt markets.

The AI buildout marks a fundamental shift in tech finance, moving companies from asset-light models to capital-intensive infrastructure plays. Oracle alone has $24 billion in data center lease commitments and issued $25.8 billion in bonds last year, while Alphabet made history with a 100-year bond issuance. Moody's highlights that much of the risk lies in off-balance-sheet obligations — $662 billion of the hyperscalers' $969 billion commitments are for future data center leases not yet reflected in debt ratios.

Investors face novel risks like rapid chip improvements potentially rendering newly built data centers obsolete within years. BlackRock notes the sector must now compensate bondholders for these uncertainties, with Meta and Alphabet already paying 10-15 basis point premiums on recent debt issuances. PIMCO warns this resembles classic overinvestment cycles that typically end in corrections.

The spending scale is staggering: Wall Street expects $100-$300 billion in AI-related bond supply this year alone, with total data center investments potentially reaching $3 trillion. This transforms tech balance sheets — while hyperscalers still have more cash than debt, their leverage ratios are creeping up as they blend operating cash flows with debt financing for the first time.

Accounting rules exacerbate the opacity, as lease commitments only appear on balance sheets when 'reasonably certain' to renew. Bond managers like Nuveen now treat these leases as de facto debt when assessing credit risk. The shift also changes valuation math — Mittal at PIMCO notes asset-heavy models traditionally command lower multiples than the tech sector's historical valuations.

China imposes export controls on rare earths to Europe in retaliation for Russia sanctions

China has imposed export controls on rare earth materials to Europe, targeting 14 European entities including defense contractors and industrial manufacturers. The move comes in direct retaliation for the EU's recent sanctions against Chinese firms accused of supporting Russia's war effort in Ukraine, escalating trade tensions between the economic powers.

The Chinese Commerce Ministry announced the restrictions on Friday, specifically barring exports of dual-use items - materials and technologies that can be used for both civilian and military purposes. Among the targeted companies are Germany's Rheinmetall, Europe's largest defense contractor, and Czech vehicle manufacturer Tatra Trucks.

This marks China's latest use of its dominant position in the global rare earth market as geopolitical leverage. China currently controls over 80% of the world's rare earth production, which are crucial for defense systems, electronics, and green energy technologies.

The export controls follow the EU's 14th package of sanctions against Russia, which included restrictions on several Chinese firms alleged to be assisting Russia's military efforts. China had previously warned it would retaliate against what it calls 'unjustified sanctions'.

Industry analysts note these rare earth controls could significantly impact Europe's defense contractors and electric vehicle manufacturers, given their reliance on Chinese supplies for key components like permanent magnets. The EU has been working to diversify its rare earth sources, but such efforts remain in early stages.

US measles cases surge past 2025 total amid vaccination decline

The US has recorded 2,318 measles cases in 2026, surpassing last year's total of 2,289 with five months remaining. Health officials attribute the rise to declining vaccination rates, particularly in vulnerable communities, jeopardizing herd immunity. Measles outbreaks are now active in 43 states, marking the worst year for the disease since 1991.

The measles resurgence highlights a worrying trend: national vaccination rates for kindergartners fell to 92.5% in the 2024-2025 school year, below the 95% threshold needed for herd immunity. Major outbreaks are concentrated in South Carolina, Utah, and Arizona, with South Carolina alone reporting 997 cases—the largest outbreak in 35 years. These outbreaks are disproportionately affecting unvaccinated populations, with 93% of cases occurring among those who were not immunized.

Public health experts caution that the reported numbers likely underestimate the true scale of the crisis, as not all cases are tested or reported to healthcare providers. The CDC has documented 151 hospitalizations so far, primarily among children under 19, though no deaths have been reported. Historically, measles was eliminated in the US in 2000, but declining vaccination rates and vaccine skepticism have allowed the virus to reemerge.

The sharp increase in cases has strained local healthcare systems, particularly in areas like South Carolina’s Upstate region, where pediatricians describe the disease as 'the flu on steroids.' Adults have also been significantly affected, with Pennsylvania reporting that 61% of cases occurred in individuals over 18. Health officials warn that unless vaccination rates improve, the country risks losing its measles elimination status and facing further outbreaks.
