International

US and Iran exchange strikes amid tensions over Strait of Hormuz

The US and Iran exchanged missile and drone strikes on Sunday, with Iran targeting US facilities in multiple Gulf states and declaring the Strait of Hormuz closed to traffic. The clashes escalate tensions over the critical waterway, which handles 20% of the world's oil shipments.

Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz marks a significant escalation in its maritime strategy. Tehran demands ships use a northern route through its territorial waters, bypassing the southern route protected by the US. This move follows repeated attacks on commercial vessels, including the M/V GFS Galaxy, which suffered significant damage in an Iranian strike.

US President Donald Trump insisted the strait remains open, emphasizing that US forces are positioned to ensure freedom of navigation. This standoff reflects underlying disputes over the June 17 memorandum of understanding, which failed to clearly define transit routes or management protocols for the strait.

The strikes also highlight Iran's use of China's BeiDou-3 satellite navigation system, which has rendered US electronic warfare jamming ineffective. This technological shift allows Iranian missiles to achieve previously unattainable precision, complicating US and allied defensive efforts.

Oman has emerged as a key mediator, proposing navigational fees for the strait as a potential compromise. However, US technical negotiators were notably absent from recent discussions in Muscat, underscoring the diplomatic complexities.

Former Emir of Qatar Sheikh Hamad bin Khalifa Al Thani Dies at 74

Sheikh Hamad bin Khalifa Al Thani, the former ruler credited with transforming Qatar into a global economic powerhouse, has died at the age of 74. His reign, marked by significant economic development and controversies including Qatar's World Cup bid, reshaped the nation's global standing.

Sheikh Hamad seized power in 1995 through a bloodless coup while his father was abroad, marking a pivotal turn in Qatar's history. Under his leadership, Qatar leveraged its natural gas reserves to become a leading LNG exporter, fundamentally altering its economic trajectory. Beyond economics, Sheikh Hamad fostered Qatar's soft power through initiatives like the launch of Al Jazeera in 1996 and strategic global investments via the Qatar Investment Authority, including acquisitions such as Harrods and Paris Saint-Germain.

The awarding of the 2022 World Cup to Qatar under his leadership remains one of the most consequential yet contentious decisions of his rule, drawing international scrutiny over labor practices and human rights. Despite these controversies, Qatar was ultimately cleared of corruption allegations related to the bid, though the event underscored the challenges of balancing rapid development with global expectations.

Sheikh Hamad's legacy also includes his role as a mediator in regional conflicts, notably in Palestine, where he supported reconciliation efforts. His voluntary abdication in 2013, handing power to his son Sheikh Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani, was a rare example of peaceful leadership transition in the Gulf region. As Qatar mourns his passing, his influence on the nation's modernization and global footprint remains deeply felt.

Iran's BeiDou-guided missiles evade US defenses in multi-nation strike

Iran launched its broadest single-night attack of the 2026 war, targeting US bases in Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Jordan with BeiDou-3 satellite-guided missiles that bypassed US electronic jamming defenses. The strikes mark a strategic shift as China's encrypted navigation system renders traditional countermeasures ineffective, forcing reliance on depleted interceptor stockpiles.

The IRGC's use of BeiDou-3's military-grade B3A signal represents a technological leap, maintaining 98% navigation accuracy under heavy jamming — a capability GPS-guided missiles lacked. This upgrade reduces Zolfaghar missile circular error to 5-10 meters, transforming them into precision weapons against fixed installations.

US interceptor systems face unsustainable attrition: Patriot and THAAD stockpiles are nearing exhaustion, with consumption rates outpacing production by 132-to-1. Each $6M Patriot fired at a $50K Shahed drone exemplifies the asymmetric cost imbalance Iran's strategy exploits.

The strikes targeted critical logistics nodes, including Duqm port's carrier-support infrastructure in Oman — a rare attack outside the Strait of Hormuz. This expands the conflict's geographical scope while avoiding direct oil shipment disruptions ahead of July 17 sanctions deadlines.

Iran's 80+ wave attrition campaign deliberately stretches allied defense inventories. With thousands of missiles remaining and decentralized production, Tehran can sustain this tempo longer than coalition forces can maintain interceptor replenishment.

The UAE's activation of national air defenses — previously rare in this conflict — signals growing regional autonomy in countering Iranian threats as US system reliability declines. This may accelerate Gulf states' investments in indigenous missile defense technologies.

Senator Lindsey Graham dies at 71, leaving political vacuum

Sen. Lindsey Graham (R-SC) died unexpectedly on July 11 from a sudden illness at age 71. Graham had served in the Senate since 2002 and was running for reelection. Under South Carolina law, Gov. Henry McMaster must appoint a replacement until a special election is held on August 11 to fill the remainder of Graham's term.

Implications

Graham's death reduces the Republican Senate majority to 52-47, complicating passage of key legislation like the SAVE America Act. His absence also removes a key Trump ally during a critical election year. The special election to replace him will test Republican unity in a traditionally red state.

How different sides see it

Republicans: Republicans, including former President Trump, praised Graham as a 'true American Patriot' and key ally who championed conservative causes like election security and strong national defense. They emphasize his late-career loyalty to Trump despite early criticisms.

Democrats: Democrats acknowledged Graham's bipartisan work on issues like Ukraine aid and Russia sanctions while noting his controversial shifts from Trump critic to staunch supporter. Some see an opportunity to challenge Republican Senate control in the upcoming special election.

International leaders: Ukrainian President Zelenskyy and Israeli PM Netanyahu highlighted Graham's support for their countries, with Zelenskyy noting his 10 visits to Ukraine during the war. Both framed him as a champion of global democracy.

Apple's AI chip push stems from abandoned car project, accelerates M7 Ultra with 1.5TB RAM support

Apple is fast-tracking its M7 chip series after scrapping Pro/Max variants of the M6, prioritizing AI hardware originally developed for its defunct self-driving car project. The M7 Ultra, due by 2028, will support up to 1.5TB RAM and anchor a new server product, marking Apple's pivot to on-device AI as a core strategy.

The Neural Engine's origins trace back to 2017's A11 Bionic chip, where it powered FaceID and AR features. Apple repurposed this automotive-grade AI architecture into consumer devices after shelving its car initiative, giving Macs a head start in local AI processing despite slower software development.

A $30B multi-year deal with Broadcom secures domestic production of AI-optimized chips through 2026, skipping planned M6 Pro/Max tiers to focus resources on M7 development. The base M6 will debut in late 2026 with 200GB/s memory bandwidth — 30% faster than M5 — but only for entry-level Macs and iPads.

The M7 lineup introduces architectural changes specifically for AI workloads, including a redesigned GPU with up to 12 cores and memory bandwidth reaching 240GB/s. Its Ultra variant's unprecedented 1.5TB RAM capacity targets data center-scale tasks while maintaining Apple's privacy-focused on-device processing paradigm.

This shift abandons Apple's traditional three-tier chip release cycle for the first time since the M1, with Pro/Max variants now delayed until late 2027. High-end Mac users face a 2+ year wait for upgrades after the M5 Ultra's late 2026 release in Mac Studio.

Local opposition and political battles slow AI data center expansion

Protests and regulatory challenges are delaying at least 75 U.S. AI data center projects worth $130 billion, as communities raise concerns about energy costs, environmental impact, and economic fairness. Political divisions are emerging, with bipartisan bills proposing construction pauses and cost protections while Trump pushes accelerated development to compete with China.

The backlash extends beyond typical NIMBY concerns, with a Milltown Partners survey showing opposition correlates more strongly with distrust of Big Tech and AI than proximity to projects. Nearly half of surveyed voters in states with active developments expressed skepticism about whether local communities would benefit economically from the facilities.

Energy demands are becoming a flashpoint, with commercial electricity use projected to exceed residential consumption for the first time in 2026 due to data centers. The EIA forecasts this demand could double by 2027, putting pressure on grids already strained in states like Virginia where existing centers have raised consumer costs.

State-level responses vary widely: Florida now prohibits cost pass-through to ratepayers, Idaho restricts water usage, and Washington eliminated tax incentives. This patchwork contrasts with federal proposals like Senator Markey's bill requiring pre-construction certification of environmental and economic standards.

The industry faces a trust deficit, as incidents like Meta's contaminated water discharge in Wyoming fuel perceptions that tech companies prioritize growth over community impacts. Protesters have successfully halted projects using diverse legal strategies, from coastal protection laws in Delaware to utility rate challenges in Wisconsin.

Political dynamics are shifting ahead of midterms, with some Republicans breaking from Trump's pro-development stance. The Ratepayer Protection Act has gained bipartisan support, reflecting constituent anger over utility bills, while progressive Democrats push for moratoriums tied to environmental reviews.

Google invests billions to challenge Nvidia's AI chip dominance with TPUs

Google is deploying billions in financial guarantees and partnerships to promote its Tensor Processing Units (TPUs) as an alternative to Nvidia's AI chips. The company is backing data-center projects like the $3.2B Lake Mariner cluster, which will lease computing power to AI firm Anthropic, signaling a direct challenge to Nvidia's 90% market share.

Google's TPU development traces back to 2013, when Jeff Dean recognized the need for specialized hardware to handle AI workloads at scale. The chips were initially used internally for services like speech recognition before being offered via Google Cloud as AI demand surged.

The company is employing a Nvidia-like strategy: financing data-center construction while ensuring tenants use its chips. Beyond Lake Mariner, Google is backing projects like the $7B River Bend development in Louisiana and a $1.4B facility in Texas.

Early adopters like Citadel Securities report 30% cost savings and 4x speed gains with TPUs. However, Nvidia's CUDA software ecosystem and hardware dominance remain significant barriers, with CEO Jensen Huang dismissing TPUs' cost advantages.

Google has reorganized its TPU efforts under AI infrastructure lead Amin Vahdat, who reports directly to Sundar Pichai. The company plans to raise $85B in equity for AI infrastructure, betting that market demand will outstrip Nvidia's supply capacity.

The strategy faces skepticism from cloud providers wary of 'Jensen jail' — Nvidia's alleged practice of favoring customers who exclusively use its chips. Google counters that AI's compute needs are vast enough for multiple players to thrive.

S&P 500 CAPE Ratio Hits Dot-Com Bubble Levels, Signaling Market Caution

The S&P 500 Shiller CAPE ratio has consistently stayed above 40 since May 2026, a level previously seen only during the dot-com bubble, suggesting the market may be overvalued. Investor pessimism is rising, with 42% expecting a market decline in the next six months. This rare valuation metric historically precedes significant market downturns, urging caution among investors.

The Shiller CAPE ratio, which tracks the S&P 500’s 10-year inflation-adjusted earnings, has averaged around 17 historically but is now exceeding 40 for the first time since the dot-com bubble. Historically, periods where the CAPE ratio surpassed 30—like in 1929, 2000, and 2020—were followed by market declines of 20% or more. For example, the Nasdaq lost 78% of its value after the 2000 peak.

Investor sentiment reflects this unease, with a recent survey showing only 31% expect stock prices to rise in the next six months, while 42% anticipate a fall. This divergence underscores growing concerns about market sustainability.

Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway has also signaled caution, with a record $397 billion in cash and Treasury bills, its highest-ever cash position. Buffett’s strategy often involves accumulating cash when bargains are scarce, suggesting he sees limited opportunities at current valuations.

Despite recent strong quarterly performance—with the S&P 500, Nasdaq, and Dow up 14%, 19%, and 13%, respectively, since April—high valuations remain a risk. Stocks trading at premiums without strong fundamentals are particularly vulnerable to pullbacks.

While historical precedent doesn’t guarantee a crash, prolonged periods of high CAPE ratios have consistently led to market corrections. Investors are advised to focus on quality stocks with solid fundamentals and prepare for potential volatility.

Biotech

US biotechs adopt extreme secrecy to counter Chinese competition

US biotech firms are increasingly withholding key details about their drug pipelines to prevent Chinese competitors from copying their innovations. Companies are delaying patent filings, using code names, and limiting disclosures at conferences to protect their intellectual property. This heightened secrecy reflects growing concerns about China's ability to rapidly reverse-engineer and outpace Western biotech advances.

The Chinese biopharma industry has demonstrated remarkable speed in developing fast-follower drugs, leveraging cheaper labor and lower costs for reagents and animal testing. US companies are particularly vulnerable because drug targets and genes cannot be patented—only the drugs themselves. This has led to extreme measures, such as Alnylam Pharmaceuticals delaying target disclosures until clinical trials and startups using code names for genes even within their own offices.

China's agility in optimizing experimental therapies has forced US biotechs to rethink their entire approach to intellectual property. Some are considering shifting to a first-to-invent patent system or making filings classified, similar to defense industry practices. The Biotechnology Innovation Organization (BIO) has even received FBI briefings on Chinese espionage, highlighting the seriousness of the threat.

RNA-based medicines, like siRNA therapies, are especially susceptible to copying, as knowing the target sequence is often enough to develop a competing drug. This has led to a broader industry trend of keeping targets and mechanisms of action confidential, even in partnerships with major pharma companies. The secrecy comes at a time when US government funding for basic research—long a driver of biotech innovation—is declining.
