Israel kills 5 Palestinians in Gaza despite ceasefire, as Netanyahu faces election pressure

Israeli attacks killed at least five Palestinians in Gaza on Thursday, violating an October ceasefire that has seen over 1,100 Palestinian deaths since its signing. The ongoing strikes and aid restrictions highlight the agreement's failure as Prime Minister Netanyahu risks losing power in upcoming elections.

The October ceasefire agreement, brokered by the U.S., was meant to halt hostilities and allow humanitarian aid into Gaza. However, Israeli restrictions have kept aid at pre-war levels, exacerbating the humanitarian crisis while reconstruction remains blocked by Netanyahu's government.

Netanyahu's political survival strategy appears tied to continued military action, with analysts suggesting he may escalate attacks ahead of October elections. His coalition has prevented all reconstruction efforts despite Gaza needing an estimated $70 billion and 10 years to rebuild.

The strikes targeted diverse locations including a tent encampment for displaced persons and a car in Khan Younis, demonstrating Israel's continued operations across the entire enclave. Palestinian health officials report these attacks follow a near-daily pattern of ceasefire violations.

Israel's international standing has deteriorated during Netanyahu's tenure, with growing allegations of war crimes in Gaza, Lebanon and Iran. The failed strategy against Hamas and economic fallout from the Iran conflict have created rare public divisions with U.S. leadership.

Canada wildfires spread smoke to U.S., triggering health alerts

More than 800 active wildfires in Canada, mostly uncontrolled, are causing hazardous air quality across northern U.S. states, with Detroit recording the world's worst pollution levels. Michigan lawmakers criticized Canada's wildfire management as the smoke disrupts daily life and threatens visibility for Sunday's World Cup final in New Jersey.

The smoke has particularly affected the Great Lakes region, with Detroit's air quality ranked worst globally by IQAir, surpassing even heavily polluted cities like Beijing. This marks the third consecutive year that U.S. Midwest states have faced severe smoke impacts from Canadian wildfires.

Climate scientists note a shift in wildfire patterns, with eastern Canadian provinces like Ontario and Quebec now experiencing large fires that were historically concentrated in western regions. This eastward expansion increases smoke exposure for densely populated areas like Toronto and New York.

Ontario's thunderstorms are unlikely to significantly dampen the fires, as drought conditions persist. Firefighting efforts prioritize protecting communities over extinguishing remote blazes, a strategy constrained by the fires' unprecedented scale and intensity.

The smoke plume's trajectory threatens to affect Sunday's World Cup final in New Jersey, though forecasts suggest winds may shift the haze toward Quebec by Monday. New York has already activated emergency air quality protocols, distributing masks and opening cooling centers.

Diplomatic tensions emerged as Michigan Republicans accused Canada of inaction, while Canadian officials emphasized bilateral cooperation and climate change mitigation. Over 150 Ontario fire crews are deployed, with evacuations ongoing in First Nations communities where structures have been destroyed.

Italian officials sentenced for deadly Genoa bridge collapse

Former Autostrade per l'Italia CEO Giovanni Castellucci was sentenced to 12 years in prison for the 2018 Morandi bridge collapse in Genoa that killed 43 people. Thirty-two others were convicted, while 25 were acquitted or had charges dropped due to a statute of limitations. The verdict marks a crucial step in assigning responsibility for one of Italy's worst infrastructure disasters.

The Morandi bridge collapse occurred during a rainstorm in August 2018, sending vehicles plummeting to the ground. Prosecutors argued that delayed maintenance and ignored warning signs led to the disaster, while defense lawyers cited design flaws, particularly a concrete-encased cable, as the cause. The trial involved over 280 hearings and lasted four years, with defendants ranging from engineers to transport ministry officials. Among those convicted was Antonino Galatà, former CEO of maintenance firm Spea, who received a five-and-a-half-year sentence. The new San Giorgio bridge, designed by Renzo Piano, replaced the collapsed structure in 2020 and features sail-like pillars honoring Genoa's maritime heritage.

White House teleprompter operator under investigation for alleged insider trading on prediction markets

Federal regulators are investigating Gabriel Perez, a White House teleprompter operator for President Trump, for allegedly making nearly $100,000 on the prediction market site Kalshi by betting on words Trump would say in public speeches. Kalshi froze Perez’s $90,000 in profits and banned him after detecting suspicious trading activity linked to his access to presidential remarks. The Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) is in settlement talks with Perez.

Implications

The case could lead to tighter scrutiny of prediction markets and their vulnerability to insider trading, particularly involving government employees. If proven, this could prompt new regulations or restrictions on federal employees' participation in such markets. It may also affect public trust in the integrity of White House operations and prediction markets.

How different sides see it

Democrats: Democrats argue that the case highlights unethical behavior and potential exploitation of presidential access for personal gain. They are calling for stricter enforcement of ethics rules to prevent future abuses.

Republicans: Republicans emphasize that the investigation is ongoing and that Perez should be presumed innocent until proven guilty. Some suggest the case is being overblown by political opponents.

CFTC and regulators: The CFTC is reportedly in settlement talks with Perez, indicating they may pursue civil enforcement rather than criminal charges. The focus is on deterring similar misconduct in prediction markets.

BP shuts corporate venture arm after 20 years

BP is closing its corporate venture arm and selling stakes in over 10 portfolio companies to Nordic private equity firm Verdane. The move marks BP's exit from climate tech investments as it refocuses on its core oil and gas business.

BP Ventures, launched in 2007, had invested in diverse sectors including green hydrogen, e-mobility, and geothermal energy, but its portfolio has struggled to deliver financial returns. Last year, the unit's investments were valued at $1.2 billion, roughly equal to its total capital deployed since inception. BP will retain stakes in a small number of companies where technology could benefit its core business, but the specific assets were not disclosed. The announcement follows BP's broader strategic reset under CEO Meg O'Neill, who has prioritized simplifying the company's portfolio and tightening capital spending. The sale to Verdane is expected to be finalized by Q2 2027, and layoffs of BP Ventures' remaining team are likely, though BP declined to comment on specific workforce impacts.

Uber nears $14.8B Delivery Hero deal to expand global footprint

Uber has agreed to acquire Delivery Hero for €41.50 per share, valuing the deal at $14.8 billion, which would nearly double its market reach to 99 countries. The acquisition hinges on regulatory approvals and a 50% shareholder acceptance threshold, with Prosus committing its 17% stake. This move positions Uber as the largest food delivery platform outside China, directly competing with DoorDash and Just Eat.

The transaction includes a carve-out of 14 overlapping markets, sold to SSW Partners for $1.6 billion, to streamline operations and avoid antitrust conflicts. These markets include Delivery Hero's presence in Austria, Poland, Spain, and others where Uber Eats already operates. SSW will independently manage these assets, seeking strategic partnerships for their long-term growth.

Uber's cross-platform strategy is central to the deal's rationale: users who engage with both ride-hailing and delivery services generate three times the gross bookings of single-product users. The combined entity projects $236 billion in pro-forma gross bookings for 2025, more than double DoorDash's $102 billion in the same period.

Regulatory scrutiny is expected, particularly in the EU and Asia, where both companies have significant overlap. Uber has pledged not to implement a Domination and Profit Transfer Agreement for three years, a concession likely aimed at easing antitrust concerns by preserving some operational independence for Delivery Hero.

The deal is structured to be accretive to Uber's non-GAAP earnings immediately upon closing, with high-single-digit percentage accretion projected within three years. Uber will fund the acquisition through existing cash reserves and new debt, while maintaining its investment-grade credit rating and share buyback program.

As part of the agreement, Uber committed to invest €2 billion in Germany over five years, focusing on autonomous vehicle development and workforce expansion. This aligns with Delivery Hero's Berlin headquarters and may help secure German regulatory approval by demonstrating long-term local investment.

UnitedHealth Group reports Q2 earnings beat but signals rising employer healthcare costs

UnitedHealth Group surpassed Q2 earnings expectations, with adjusted EPS of $6.38 beating estimates of $4.90, and raised its full-year profit outlook. However, the company warned that rising healthcare costs will continue to pressure employers, potentially forcing more small businesses to drop traditional health benefits for workers. This comes as UnitedHealth faces declining membership in its Affordable Care Act exchange and Medicare Advantage plans due to affordability concerns.

UnitedHealth's Q2 results highlight a broader trend in employer-sponsored healthcare, where rising medical costs are increasingly shifting financial burdens onto businesses. The company reported a medical benefit ratio of 86.7%, indicating improved profitability, but CFO Wayne DeVeydt noted that commercial medical costs remain elevated, running above 11%. This is driven in part by higher provider billing intensity and the No Surprises Act’s independent dispute resolution process, which affects commercial insurance plans.

About 150 million Americans rely on employer-sponsored insurance, but UnitedHealth’s earnings call suggested that small businesses, in particular, may struggle to sustain these benefits. Executives emphasized that affordability pressures are unlikely to ease soon, projecting continued membership losses in ACA exchange and Medicare Advantage plans. The company expects to lose 500,000 ACA members and 1.1 million Medicare Advantage members in 2026.

Despite these challenges, UnitedHealth is leveraging AI to streamline operations and improve efficiency, investing $1.5 billion in the technology to accelerate processes like prior authorizations and fraud detection. The company believes these efforts will help stabilize margins amid declining enrollment and rising costs. Yet, DeVeydt cautioned that the turnaround remains a 'multiyear journey,' with broader implications for employers and the healthcare system.

Mighty Therapeutics secures $150M funding for mitochondrial disease treatments

Mighty Therapeutics announced $150 million in financing to advance treatments for mitochondrial dysfunction in diseases including Parkinson's and macular degeneration. The funding includes a $125 million credit facility and $25 million Series B investment, extending the company's runway to 2028. This bolsters development of its FDA-approved Barth syndrome treatment and pipeline candidates targeting rare and age-related conditions.

The funding follows Mighty Therapeutics' September 2025 FDA approval of elamipretide for Barth syndrome - the first ever mitochondrial-targeted therapy. The company's pipeline includes bevemipretide for neurological conditions and two preclinical assets for rare disorders.

K2 HealthVentures' credit facility is milestone-driven, with subsequent tranches tied to clinical and commercial achievements. This structure aligns investor risk with developmental progress in high-need therapeutic areas.

Mitochondrial dysfunction underlies both rare genetic disorders and common age-related diseases. Mighty's approach of directly targeting cellular energy mechanisms represents a growing frontier in biotech, as evidenced by competing Parkinson's programs from Gain Therapeutics and Oryon.

The Parkinson's disease field is seeing parallel advances, with Gain Therapeutics recently receiving FDA clearance for a Phase 2 trial of oral therapy GT-02287. This treatment targets protein clumping via a different mechanism than Mighty's mitochondrial approach.

Mighty's financing coincides with increased investor interest in neurodegeneration therapies, with Oryon also emerging from stealth with $21 million for Parkinson's research. The company's extended runway allows it to capitalize on its first-mover advantage in mitochondrial medicine.
