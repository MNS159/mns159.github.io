At least 28 killed in China shoe factory fire, many trapped

A fire engulfed a shoe factory in Jinjiang, China, killing at least 28 people and trapping workers on the roof. President Xi Jinping ordered an all-out rescue effort and accountability for those responsible, as flammable materials fueled one of China's deadliest industrial accidents this year.

The blaze broke out around noon on July 9 at the Huiteng Shoes factory in Fujian province, a major hub for global athletic footwear production. Over 180 firefighters battled the inferno for hours, struggling to reach upper floors where stairwells were blocked by shoe materials. Video showed workers signaling for help from the rooftop as black smoke poured from lower floors.

Preliminary investigations suggest the fire started on the ground floor, accelerated by adhesives and other flammable materials used in shoe manufacturing. The factory owner and several managers have been detained, with company accounts frozen as authorities investigate safety violations.

This marks China's second major industrial tragedy in two months, following a May fireworks factory explosion that killed 37. Despite a November 2024 nationwide fire safety campaign launched after Hong Kong's deadly high-rise blaze (168 killed), enforcement remains spotty in manufacturing centers where production often outweighs safety concerns.

President Xi's immediate call for accountability reflects growing pressure over workplace safety. China recorded 12,804 workplace deaths in just the first nine months of 2024, with Fujian province's dense manufacturing zones particularly prone to accidents due to crowded conditions and lax oversight.

The incident exposes persistent gaps in China's industrial safety reforms. While Jinjiang's factories produce footwear for global brands, many operate with outdated infrastructure. This tragedy may spur tighter inspections, but systemic change requires addressing corruption in local enforcement agencies that enable safety shortcuts.

Roche ends two Huntington’s disease drug programmes after disappointing results

Roche has discontinued two clinical programs for Huntington’s disease, halting development of the drugs tominersen and RG6496 due to lack of efficacy and safety concerns in long-term dosing. This marks a significant setback in the search for treatments for the neurodegenerative disorder.

Roche announced the end of its GENERATION HD2 trial for tominersen, a drug designed to lower levels of the huntingtin protein. Despite showing biomarker improvements, the drug failed to slow disease progression or improve symptoms in early Huntington’s patients, leading to its discontinuation. Separately, Roche also halted the POINT-HD trial for RG6496, another huntingtin-lowering therapy, after animal studies indicated it wouldn’t be suitable for long-term dosing.

Tominersen had been a promising therapy, developed in partnership with Ionis Pharmaceuticals, and had shown early success in reducing huntingtin protein levels. However, the lack of clinical efficacy in phase trials led to its termination. RG6496, designed to selectively target expanded huntingtin, was also discontinued based on preclinical findings.

Despite these setbacks, Roche emphasized that neither decision was driven by safety concerns, and no major adverse effects were observed in participants. The company remains committed to Huntington’s research, focusing on other programs like its gene therapy RG6662. This development leaves a gap in the pipeline for Huntington’s treatments, but other companies like Wave Life Sciences and Takeda continue to explore antisense therapies.

The discontinuation of these programs highlights the challenges in treating neurodegenerative diseases and underscores the need for continued innovation in Huntington’s research.
