U.S. and Iran escalate attacks, threatening Strait of Hormuz shipping

The U.S. and Iran exchanged new strikes after three commercial ships were attacked near the Strait of Hormuz, with President Trump declaring the June ceasefire 'over.' Iran threatened to close the vital waterway again, risking global oil supplies that transit the chokepoint.

The latest escalation began when Iran attacked three commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz between July 6-7, including a Qatari LNG tanker and Saudi oil supertanker. This followed two earlier attacks in late June. The U.S. responded with strikes on Iranian coastal cities and later Tehran, while Iran launched missiles at U.S. bases in Bahrain and Kuwait.

The Strait of Hormuz normally handles 20% of global oil consumption - about 20 million barrels daily. While 513 ships transited since the June 17 ceasefire, traffic remains at just 28 ships/day compared to the pre-war average of 100. About 6,000 seafarers remain stranded in the region.

A key dispute centers on shipping routes. Vessels are avoiding central channels believed to be mined, instead using new corridors through Omani or Iranian waters. Iran insists ships must use its designated routes and plans to introduce transit fees, which the U.S. rejects as 'unacceptable.'

The renewed fighting threatens to completely close the strait again, as happened during the February-June conflict. Global energy markets remain on edge, with oil prices already volatile from months of disruption to this critical chokepoint.

The situation remains precarious as Iran continues funeral ceremonies for Ayatollah Khamenei while conducting military operations. With both sides hardening positions and Trump declaring the truce over, analysts warn the conflict could escalate into full-scale war.

Factory fire in China's 'shoe capital' kills 28

A fire at a shoe factory in Jinjiang, China, killed at least 28 people, with dramatic footage showing trapped workers on the roof. President Xi Jinping called for accountability, as the city produces 20% of the world's sports shoes. This follows a recent push for fire safety reforms after a deadly Hong Kong blaze.

The fire broke out at the Huiteng Footwear factory in Jinjiang, Fujian province, a hub for global shoe manufacturing. Initial reports suggest flammable materials stored on the ground floor may have ignited the blaze, which quickly engulfed the building. Rescue efforts evacuated over 200 people, but the death toll remains at 28 with injuries still unconfirmed.

Chinese authorities have detained several employees linked to the factory's ownership and frozen the company's bank accounts, signaling a swift response to potential negligence. This comes amid heightened scrutiny of industrial safety after a November Hong Kong apartment fire killed 168 people, prompting nationwide fire prevention campaigns.

Jinjiang's prominence as China's 'shoe capital' — producing one-fifth of the world's sports shoes — underscores the economic stakes of the disaster. The incident may accelerate existing regulatory pressures on manufacturers in the region, where rapid industrialization has sometimes outpaced safety standards.

President Xi Jinping's direct intervention, demanding accountability, reflects the political sensitivity of industrial accidents in China. The government's rapid containment of financial assets and personnel suggests a strategy to preempt public outrage, a pattern seen after past disasters.

The tragedy coincides with broader challenges in China's disaster response, as globaltimes.cn reports Xi urging intensified flood relief efforts elsewhere in the country. The simultaneous crises test the capacity of local and national emergency systems.

Supreme Court to Hear Climate Case Challenging State Lawsuits Against Oil Companies

The U.S. Supreme Court has agreed to hear _Suncor Energy Inc. v. County Commissioners of Boulder County_, a case challenging Colorado’s ability to sue oil companies under state law for damages allegedly caused by climate change. The case centers on whether federal environmental laws preempt Boulder’s state-law claims. Oral arguments are expected in the fall, with a decision likely in 2026.

Implications

The case could determine whether state and municipal climate lawsuits against fossil fuel companies can proceed in state courts. It also raises questions about the balance of federal versus state authority in regulating interstate pollution and energy policy. The outcome may influence similar lawsuits across the country and the legal protections available to energy companies.

How different sides see it

Democrats: Democrats and Boulder officials argue that states have the right to hold companies accountable for local damages caused by climate change. They contend that federal laws should not preempt state efforts to address environmental harms and seek compensation for affected communities.

Republicans: Republicans and oil companies assert that federal law should preempt state climate lawsuits to avoid a patchwork of conflicting regulations. They argue that interstate pollution and energy policy are federal matters and that state courts lack jurisdiction over such cases.

Federal Government: The federal government has filed a brief supporting the oil companies, arguing that Colorado cannot apply its laws to companies’ out-of-state conduct. It emphasizes the need for uniform federal regulation of interstate pollution and energy issues.

Meta's new AI chips to begin production in September

Meta plans to start production of its latest AI-specific chips in September, aiming to reduce reliance on GPUs from Nvidia and AMD. The chips, developed under its MTIA program, will handle AI training and inference tasks across its platforms, as the company seeks to secure computing capacity for its AI initiatives.

Meta is pushing forward with its in-house AI chip production to lessen its dependency on external GPU providers like Nvidia and AMD. The chips, part of the Meta Training and Inference Accelerator (MTIA) program, are set to begin production in September. Developed with Broadcom and manufactured by TSMC, these chips will focus on AI training and inference tasks, particularly for Meta's ranking and recommendation algorithms.

Meta's move comes amid an unprecedented component shortage and skyrocketing GPU costs. The company has been securing long-term supply agreements with Samsung for RAM, Sandisk for storage, and Sumitomo Electric for fiber-optic equipment to support its AI infrastructure. Meta plans to deploy 7 gigawatts of computing capacity this year and double that by next year.

The MTIA chips are designed with a modular approach to adapt to rapidly evolving AI workloads. Meta has been producing AI chips since 2023, and the latest generation builds on previous iterations, incorporating new hardware technologies and insights from AI workloads. While Meta still expects to spend heavily on GPUs from Nvidia and AMD, the in-house chips could help trim costs at the margins.

Meta's AI infrastructure spending is part of a broader trend among tech giants to develop custom AI chips. Companies like Amazon, Google, and OpenAI are also investing in proprietary silicon to reduce reliance on Nvidia. Meta's efforts are central to its strategy of scaling AI capabilities across its platforms, including Facebook and Instagram, while managing the financial and logistical challenges of deploying cutting-edge AI technologies.

EU unveils action plan to harness AI for cybersecurity while mitigating risks

The European Commission presented a new action plan to leverage AI for cybersecurity while addressing its risks, including automated attacks and vulnerability exploitation. The plan establishes evaluation protocols for AI models, creates testing platforms, and launches an EU Grand Challenge to develop cybersecurity solutions.

The EU's action plan focuses on three key objectives: promoting safe AI use, reinforcing cybersecurity resilience, and scaling Europe's AI capabilities for cyber defense. It mandates pre-market risk assessments for advanced AI models under the AI Act and creates a secure testing platform through ENISA and the Joint Research Centre.

A centerpiece is the 'EU Grand Challenge on AI for cybersecurity,' which will pool resources from companies, researchers, and organizations to develop innovative defenses. The plan also establishes structured access protocols to advanced AI models for both public and private entities.

The initiative builds on existing EU regulations including the AI Act, Cyber Resilience Act, and NIS2 Directive. It emphasizes 'security by design' principles and urges critical sectors like energy and healthcare to adopt AI-powered vulnerability detection.

Notably, the plan acknowledges AI's dual-use risks—while it can accelerate threat detection, malicious actors could use the same technology to automate attacks at unprecedented scale. The Commission will work with ENISA to develop countermeasures against AI-driven cyber threats.

Funding will come through existing programs like AI Factories and the Tech Sovereignty Package. The plan aligns with the EU's broader push for 'technological sovereignty,' reducing dependence on non-European AI systems while fostering homegrown innovation.

ARPA-H launches $160M program to accelerate custom gene editing therapies

The U.S. health research agency ARPA-H unveiled a $160 million initiative called THRIVE to develop tailored gene editing treatments for rare diseases. Seven research teams must begin clinical trials within three years, potentially accelerating therapies for conditions affecting multiple organ systems.

The THRIVE program marks ARPA-H's most ambitious foray into gene editing to date, focusing on rare diseases that often lack treatment options. By funding seven multidisciplinary teams simultaneously, the agency aims to create a pipeline of therapies rather than betting on a single approach.

This initiative comes amid a broader biotech resurgence, with privateequitywire.co.uk reporting record M&A activity in 2026. The sector's growth is fueled by advances in sequencing technologies like those from Renew Biotechnologies, which recently expanded epigenetic diagnostics through partnerships with Oxford Nanopore and Agilent.

The funding model reflects ARPA-H's high-risk, high-reward philosophy, requiring teams to meet aggressive clinical trial timelines. Some projects may leverage emerging tools like native-read sequencing, as demonstrated in Wasatch BioLabs' collaboration with Agilent for methylation analysis.

Success could validate gene editing's potential beyond current CRISPR applications, particularly for complex rare diseases. However, the tight three-year clinical trial deadline presents significant technical and regulatory challenges for researchers.

The program aligns with growing investor interest in precision medicine, as the biotech sector outperforms broader market volatility. THRIVE's outcomes may influence future public-private partnerships in genomic medicine.
