U.S. and Iran Exchange Intense New Attacks as Strait of Hormuz Traffic Slows

The United States and Iran launched fresh airstrikes and missile attacks Thursday after Iran struck three oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz, with President Trump declaring the fragile ceasefire “over.” The exchange has slashed tanker traffic through the strait and pushed oil prices up more than 6%, threatening to reignite a war that disrupted global energy markets and supply chains since February.

The United States launched a second round of airstrikes against Iran early Thursday, hitting 90 targets including airport runways and missile launchers, according to U.S. Central Command [apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/iran-us-israel-war-oil-july-9-2026-0472764b119d7aa204de4f7f5e44a9bf). Iran responded by firing ballistic missiles and drones at U.S. allies in the region, with sirens sounding in Bahrain, Kuwait, and Qatar, and debris injuring one person in Jordan. Tehran claimed it struck a U.S. base in Jordan, while Iranian state media reported at least 14 military personnel killed and 78 wounded in the American attacks.

The escalation follows Iran’s attack on three tankers in the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday. President Trump declared the interim ceasefire “over” on Wednesday, warning that further attacks would “get much worse” and threatening to hit Iranian civilian infrastructure and seize Kharg Island. The ceasefire, established through an April pause in fighting and extended by a June memorandum of understanding, had begun to restore oil exports through the strait, with 576 ships transiting in June compared to 233 in May [apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/iran-us-israel-war-oil-july-9-2026-0472764b119d7aa204de4f7f5e44a9bf). Under the MoU, Iran demanded ships use a northern route under its control for safe passage, while attacking vessels using a U.S.-protected southern corridor.

Tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has plummeted. On Wednesday, only 13 tankers crossed, down from an average of 33 per day in the prior week, and overnight into Thursday just five commercial ships crossed, with no outbound tankers at all [cnbc.com](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/09/iran-strait-hormuz-oil-tanker-traffic-gulf-trump.html). Analysts say they are operating under “full conflict conditions,” and oil prices have rallied more than 6% this week, though markets are not pricing in a complete closure, instead bracing for a pattern of missile skirmishes between periods of uneasy calm.

The renewed fighting underscores a divide inside Iran between hardliners who want to retain control of the strait as leverage and pragmatists seeking a permanent deal to lift sanctions and stabilize the economy. Negotiations on finalizing the ceasefire had been set to begin after the funeral of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, who was killed in the war’s opening strikes on Feb. 28 [aljazeera.com](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/9/us-iran-launch-more-attacks-as-mediators-urge-warring-sides-to-uphold-mou). Trump’s comments and the latest attacks risk scuttling those talks entirely, with Iranian officials accusing the U.S. of failing to honor its commitments.

Mediators, including Oman and Qatar, continue to urge both sides to uphold the MoU, but the rapid breakdown raises the prospect of a wider war that could halt energy shipments through the strait, through which a fifth of the world’s traded oil and natural gas passes. The U.S. has also reimposed oil sanctions on Iran, further tightening economic pressure [thehill.com](https://thehill.com/policy/defense/5960413-us-iran-strikes-intensify-donald-trump-ceasefire-over/). For now, the region remains on a knife’s edge, with both sides exchanging fire and the world watching for any further escalation.

Trump Signs Executive Orders to Accelerate Quantum Computing Development, Target 2028 for First Practical Quantum Computer

On June 22, 2026, President Trump signed two executive orders on quantum technology. The first, 'Ushering in the Next Frontier of Quantum Innovation,' establishes the Quantum Computer for Application Development and Discovery Science (QC-ADDS) effort to deliver at least one quantum computer to a Department of Energy facility by 2028. The second order accelerates the federal migration to post-quantum cryptography to 2031. The orders also direct agencies to boost domestic supply chains, workforce development, and quantum sensor deployment.

Implications

The QC-ADDS effort could give the U.S. government a dedicated quantum computer for scientific discovery, potentially spurring commercial applications and national security advances. The accelerated cryptography deadline pressures federal agencies and private industry to adopt new standards sooner. The emphasis on domestic supply chains and workforce training may reshape the quantum industry's growth trajectory and reduce reliance on foreign components.

How different sides see it

The White House: The administration argues the orders are necessary to maintain U.S. leadership in quantum information science and technology. White House Director of OSTP Michael Kratsios said the first order 'calls for the development of the first-ever quantum computer powerful enough for scientific research, initiating a new era of commercial capabilities' and that the computer could be completed by 2028. The second order 'will accelerate the migration to post-quantum cryptography to 2031,' bolstering national security.

Technology industry leaders: Executives from major tech companies, including IBM, attended the signing and voiced support. The White House highlighted that the orders aim to 'drive transformational growth' and create high-paying jobs. Industry groups see the initiatives as a catalyst for private investment and a clearer regulatory path for quantum commercialization.

Congressional Democrats: No formal Democratic response has been issued, but the orders build on the bipartisan National Quantum Initiative Act of 2018. Some Democrats may raise questions about the lack of specific funding details (the orders do not specify a budget) and the accelerated timeline for cryptography migration, which could impose costs on federal agencies and private firms.

$3.2 Trillion AI Deal-Making Frenzy Reshapes Economy

Global mergers and acquisitions hit a record $2.6–$3.2 trillion in the first half of 2026, fueled by blockbuster AI deals and a business-friendly regulatory shift in the U.S. and Europe. The surge is reshaping corporate strategy, rewarding companies that pursue scale through transformative acquisitions over organic growth.

Global M&A activity has shattered records in the first half of 2026, with deal values reaching between $2.6 trillion (Bloomberg) and $3.2 trillion (Mergermarket) — the highest six-month total on record. The surge is driven overwhelmingly by mega-deals: 38 to 47 transactions valued above $10 billion collectively accounted for nearly half of all global M&A volume, an all-time high.

Technology remained the largest M&A sector, totaling $649 billion, with AI-related acquisitions at the center. Among the largest deals were NextEra Energy's $66.8 billion merger with Dominion Energy and SpaceX's roughly $60 billion acquisition of Cursor. Deal makers say companies are not buying for the next five years, but "for the next 40 or 50 years" [thestar.com.my](https://www.thestar.com.my/business/business-news/2026/07/02/global-ma-tops-us25-trillion-after-1h-boom).

The regulatory environment has been a critical enabler. The Trump administration has taken a business-friendly stance, and European policymakers are proposing reforms to encourage regional champions. Japan is also pushing governance reforms to spur more efficient use of corporate cash [economictimes.indiatimes.com](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/markets/us-stocks/news/global-market-ma-hits-record-2-8-trillion-in-h1-2026-as-mega-deals-dominate-activity/articleshow/132128925.cms).

Cross-border M&A surged 62% year-on-year to $893 billion, with the U.S. accounting for a quarter of all cross-border transactions. Strong corporate earnings, supportive debt markets (investment-grade issuance hit a record $3.4 trillion), and abundant liquidity have all backed the wave. Corporate restructuring — including spin-offs and asset sales — also accelerated as investors grow less tolerant of diversified conglomerates.

Bankers expect momentum to continue, with a growing pipeline of deals suggesting the full year could surpass the post-pandemic peak of 2021. The core driver, across sectors, is a bet that AI-era scale, rather than agility, will define the winners of the next economic cycle.

OpenAI launches GPT-5.6 after Trump administration cleared public rollout

OpenAI will release its GPT-5.6 series — including the flagship Sol model — to the public on Thursday, July 9, after the Trump administration approved the broader launch following a 30-day voluntary testing period. The delay, which the White House requested to assess cybersecurity risks, marks the first time the government has formally intervened in a frontier AI model release, setting a precedent for future regulation of advanced AI systems.

OpenAI announced on Tuesday night that its GPT-5.6 series, comprising the flagship Sol, mid-range Terra, and low-cost Luna models, will be publicly released on Thursday. The rollout follows weeks of negotiations with the Trump administration, which asked the company in late June to limit initial access to a small group of government-approved partners after cybersecurity researchers warned of the model's unprecedented ability to identify exploitable code vulnerabilities [channelnewsasia.com](https://www.channelnewsasia.com/business/openai-launch-new-model-us-national-security-6242841).

The administration's intervention was part of a broader shift in AI policy. In June, President Trump signed an executive order calling for voluntary 30-day pre-release testing of the most powerful models, prompted partly by concerns over Anthropic's Claude Mythos and its successor Fable 5, which was pulled shortly after release due to weaker-than-advertised guardrails [politico.com](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/06/25/openai-gpt-model-goverment-approval-00977551). The White House emphasized that the GPT-5.6 approval was not an endorsement but a sign that the voluntary process worked as intended.

Inside the White House, officials were divided: some wanted a lighter regulatory touch, while others pressed for stricter oversight of potential national security risks. OpenAI initially resisted the government access process, calling it unsustainable, but ultimately cooperated to avoid a longer standoff [newsnationnow.com](https://www.newsnationnow.com/business/tech/ai/openai-chat-gpt-5-6-model-public-release-trump/). The company noted that Terra, the mid-tier model, will be priced at half the cost of GPT-5.5, aiming to lock in customers amid stiff competition from Anthropic and Google.

The episode has drawn criticism from AI policy advocates who warn that the ad-hoc approach creates uncertainty for companies racing to compete with China. Former Trump AI adviser Dean Ball, who recently joined OpenAI, predicted weeks earlier that the whole industry could face an effective freeze on public releases until the government resolves its approach [politico.com](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/06/25/openai-gpt-model-goverment-approval-00977551). With both OpenAI and Anthropic now working with Washington to formalize a repeatable process, the July 9 release will serve as a test case for how the U.S. balances innovation with security in the age of advanced AI.

Fed Minutes Show Some Officials Backed Rate Hike at June Meeting Amid Split Over Inflation

The Federal Reserve's June meeting minutes, released Wednesday, revealed that a few officials supported raising interest rates to address resurgent inflation, though the committee voted unanimously to hold rates steady. The minutes underscore deepening divisions over whether inflation will cool as the Iran war winds down or remain elevated due to AI-driven demand and tariffs. This split signals that the Fed may raise rates later this year if inflation persists, which would increase borrowing costs for consumers and businesses.

The minutes from the Federal Reserve’s June 16-17 meeting—the first under Chairman Kevin Warsh—showed that a few officials believed there was “a case for raising” the Fed’s key rate, which currently stands at 3.5 to 3.75 percent. While the decision to hold rates was unanimous, the record revealed an almost even split among policymakers about the inflation outlook: half of the 18 officials who submitted projections supported lifting rates by year-end, while the other half favored keeping them unchanged or cutting [nytimes.com](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/07/08/business/fed-meeting-minutes-warsh-interest-rates.html).

The debate centered on whether the recent surge in inflation—which hit a three-year high of 4.2% in May—is temporary or prolonged. Many officials expressed concern that massive investment in artificial intelligence infrastructure would sustain upward pressure on prices for semiconductors, computer equipment, and electricity. Others pointed to easing gas prices and fading tariff effects as reasons to expect inflation to decelerate [apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/federal-reserve-warsh-inflation-3ec0b0c2fe05e3833e324fa522a1882a).

Chairman Warsh, appointed by President Donald Trump, emphasized at his post-meeting news conference that the Fed is committed to returning inflation to its 2% target, which it has missed for over five years. He declined to submit a formal economic projection, stating that doing so can lock policymakers into a specific path. Economists and investors interpreted his remarks as a signal that a rate hike could come later this year [nytimes.com](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/07/08/business/fed-meeting-minutes-warsh-interest-rates.html).

A key concern for the Fed is whether consumer expectations for inflation will become entrenched. The New York Fed’s survey showed one-year inflation expectations rose to 3.7%, the highest in nearly three years. If households and businesses assume prices will stay elevated, that belief can become self-fulfilling, leading to higher wage demands and price increases. Most Fed officials say they closely monitor such expectations, though many put more weight on financial-market measures [apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/federal-reserve-warsh-inflation-3ec0b0c2fe05e3833e324fa522a1882a).

AstraZeneca Stock Falls 9% After Heart Drug Trial Fails

AstraZeneca shares dropped up to 9% after a late-stage trial for its heart drug Wainua failed to reduce deaths or heart-related emergencies in patients with ATTR cardiomyopathy. The miss surprised investors and analysts, who said the bigger blow may be to the company's credibility rather than its long-term sales target. The failure strengthens Alnylam's monopoly in the silencing-drug market for the condition.

AstraZeneca’s stock tumbled as much as 9% on Thursday after the company announced that a pivotal Phase III trial of Wainua (eplontersen) did not meet its primary endpoint. The drug was being tested in patients with transthyretin-mediated amyloid cardiomyopathy (ATTR-CM), a rare, life-threatening condition where misfolded proteins stiffen the heart muscle. The trial failed to show a statistically significant reduction in all-cause mortality and recurrent cardiovascular events over 140 weeks when added to standard care.

The result was a sharp surprise for a company that rarely posts negative trial data under CEO Pascal Soriot. Jefferies analysts noted that management had been “very confident” about hitting the primary endpoint, and the miss — partly attributed to the high proportion of patients already on stabilizer therapy — raises questions about trial design and credibility. The news erased about $2.5 billion in risk-adjusted sales expectations for Wainua, though AstraZeneca’s $80 billion 2030 target remains intact, according to Jefferies.

AstraZeneca’s existing license for Wainua is unaffected; the drug is already approved for ATTR polyneuropathy (nerve damage) in the U.S. and Europe. But the failure in cardiomyopathy removes it as a competitor in the rapidly growing ATTR-CM market. Pfizer’s Vyndamax and BridgeBio’s acoramidis are stabilizers, while Alnylam’s Amvuttra (the only other silencer) now has a monopoly in the silencing-drug segment. Alnylam shares jumped 15%, and BridgeBio rose 13% on the news.

The miss also weighed on the broader market: the FTSE 100 was the only major European index in the red, down 0.5%, as AstraZeneca is the UK’s largest listed company. In the U.S., shares of co-developer Ionis Pharmaceuticals fell 19%. AstraZeneca plans to present full data at the European Society of Cardiology meeting in August. For now, analysts said the stock may not recover until the next major catalyst — results from a lung cancer trial called AVANZAR — is out of the way.

ARPA-H Commits $160 Million to Seven Teams for Custom Gene Editing Therapies Targeting Rare Diseases

The Advanced Research Projects Agency for Health (ARPA-H) announced it will allocate up to $160 million through its THRIVE program to support seven teams developing in vivo precision genetic medicines for rare diseases. Each team must begin clinical trials within three years. The initiative aims to make one-time, affordable gene editing treatments widely accessible through regional treatment centers and virtual clinics, addressing the roughly 95% of rare diseases that currently lack approved therapies.

The THRIVE program (Treating Hereditary Rare Diseases with In Vivo Precision Genetic Medicines) was originally launched by ARPA-H in September 2025 through an Innovative Solutions Opening solicitation. It focuses on developing integrated platform technologies that can rapidly design and deliver gene-correcting tools directly to patients’ cells, avoiding ex vivo approaches or gene supplementation. The program is structured around three modules: platform design for rapid iteration of affordable precision genetic medicines (PGMs), investigational medicine development, and real-world viability pilots and scaling. [arpa-h.gov](https://arpa-h.gov/news-and-events/arpa-h-launches-groundbreaking-program-develop-affordable-precision-genetic)

Rare diseases collectively affect more than 30 million Americans, mostly children, and lifetime medical costs can be devastating. ARPA-H Acting Director Jason Roos stated that THRIVE aims to 'transform precision genetic medicines into universal, widely available cures,' solidifying U.S. leadership in advanced medical treatments. Program Manager Mimi Lee emphasized that the goal is for patients to receive treatment where they live, using existing regional centers and virtual clinics to keep costs manageable. [arpa-h.gov](https://arpa-h.gov/explore-funding/programs/thrive)

The new funding announcement on July 9, 2026, specifies that seven teams will be backed with up to $160 million, each targeting different organ systems or disease categories. Teams are required to initiate clinical trials by the third year of the program, though some may begin sooner. This marks a significant step toward moving PGM platforms from the lab into human testing, with the potential to demonstrate that single-intervention genetic treatments can be developed affordably and at scale. [statnews.com](https://www.statnews.com/2026/07/09/arpa-h-160-million-custom-gene-editing-funding-rare-disease/?utm_campaign=rss)

By setting a tight timeline for clinical trials and emphasizing cost optimization from the outset, THRIVE differs from earlier precision medicine efforts that often stalled due to high per-patient costs. The program’s architecture also encourages teaming across private and public entities, using Other Transactions Agreements rather than traditional grants, to speed up development and delivery.
