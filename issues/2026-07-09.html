U.S. and Iran Exchange Military Strikes Amid Ceasefire Tensions

The U.S. launched new airstrikes against Iran early Thursday, targeting 90 sites across the country, while Iran responded by firing missiles at U.S.-allied nations Kuwait, Qatar, and Bahrain. The escalation threatens a fragile interim ceasefire brokered last month and raises fears of renewed conflict in the region.

The latest round of violence began after Iran attacked three tankers in the strategically vital Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday. President Trump warned that Iranian aggression signaled the end of the ceasefire and authorized retaliatory strikes, vowing to escalate further if attacks continue. Both sides appear to be targeting critical infrastructure, with U.S. strikes potentially aimed at Iran's nuclear facilities and Iranian missiles threatening Arab nations allied with Washington.

The interim ceasefire agreement, mediated by Pakistan and Qatar in June, had gradually reopened the Strait of Hormuz to shipping traffic, providing relief to global energy markets. However, the latest exchange of fire jeopardizes these gains. Maritime traffic through the strait had rebounded to pre-war levels in June, but renewed conflict could once again restrict this crucial shipping lane.

The situation remains volatile, with conflicting signals from U.S. leadership. While Trump approved military action, he also stated the strikes did not represent a return to full-scale war. Meanwhile, Iran's leadership appears divided, with hard-liners pushing for continued control over the Strait of Hormuz and pragmatists seeking a permanent peace deal to lift sanctions.

Regional tensions are further complicated by the timing of these events. Negotiations on a permanent settlement were set to begin after the funeral of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, killed in the war's initial stages. The U.S. strikes came as Iran prepared to lay its former Supreme Leader to rest, potentially fueling nationalist sentiment.

The renewed fighting has already caused casualties, with Iran reporting 14 deaths and 78 injuries from the U.S. strikes. Kuwait intercepted Iranian missile fire, though falling debris injured one person. The situation remains fluid, with mediators urgently calling for diplomatic solutions to prevent full-scale war from reigniting.

Factory Fire in China’s 'Shoe Capital' Kills 28

A fire at a shoe factory in Jinjiang, China, killed at least 28 people, with hundreds evacuated and rescue efforts ongoing. This raises concerns about safety in China’s manufacturing hubs.

A deadly fire broke out at the Huiteng Footwear factory in Jinjiang, a major shoe manufacturing hub in China’s Fujian province, killing at least 28 people. The blaze erupted around noon local time, with hundreds of rescue workers deployed to the scene and over 200 people evacuated. Initial reports suggest the fire may have started on the ground floor where flammable materials were stored.

Jinjiang, often called China’s 'shoe capital,' produces 20% of the world’s sports shoes, making this incident a significant blow to the region’s manufacturing industry. Chinese President Xi Jinping has ordered those responsible to be held 'strictly accountable,' and authorities have detained several individuals linked to the factory’s owners while freezing the company’s bank accounts.

This tragedy comes amid renewed scrutiny of fire safety in China following a high-rise apartment blaze in Hong Kong last November that killed 168 people. The government had launched a campaign to prevent such disasters, but this incident highlights persistent risks in industrial settings.

The factory fire also underscores the challenges of balancing rapid industrial growth with safety standards in China’s manufacturing sector, which remains a critical driver of the global economy.

White House North Portico gets scaffolding and drapes for repairs

Scaffolding and drapes have been installed around the White House North Portico for restoration work on its stone columns, which were built in 1830. The White House confirmed the repairs are standard maintenance, though reports suggest President Trump has expressed interest in replacing the Ionic-style columns with more decorative Corinthian-style ones.

Implications

The restoration could preserve the historic integrity of the White House's North Portico, a visible symbol of the presidency. If the columns are replaced, it would mark a significant architectural change to the building's facade, potentially sparking debates over historical preservation versus modernization.

How different sides see it

White House: The White House states the scaffolding is for 'standard restoration work' on the stone columns, framing it as routine maintenance to preserve the historic structure.

Preservation advocates: Some preservationists argue the Ionic columns are historically significant and should be maintained as-is, citing the White House's architectural heritage. Rodney Mims Cook Jr., chair of the Commission of Fine Arts, has suggested Corinthian columns would be more fitting, calling them 'the highest order.'

Trump administration: President Trump has privately expressed dissatisfaction with the current columns, calling them outdated, and has reportedly favored replacing them with Corinthian-style pillars to align with other federal buildings' grandeur.

Palm Beach International Airport officially renamed Donald J. Trump International Airport

On July 9, 2026, the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) changed Palm Beach International Airport's locational identifier from PBI to DJT, implementing a Florida law signed by Gov. Ron DeSantis on March 30, 2026. The International Air Transport Association (IATA) code will change from PBI to DJT on August 18, 2026. The renaming does not affect airport ownership, operations, or funding sources, with transition costs covered by airport revenues or state appropriations.

Implications

The name change requires updates to aviation systems, signage, and passenger-facing materials, with phased implementation based on funding. The FAA and ICAO identifiers are now active for flight operations, while travelers must use the new IATA code for bookings after August 18. The renaming sets a precedent as the first U.S. airport named after a sitting president and involves a trademark licensing agreement with the Trump Organization.

Republicans: Supporters argue the renaming honors Trump's contributions to Florida and the nation, with Gov. DeSantis and state legislators mandating the change. Eric Trump called it a 'deserving honor,' emphasizing the family's personal connection to the airport.

Democrats: Critics, including Rep. Lois Frankel, decry the state's override of local authority and the renaming of a public facility for a sitting president. Two lawsuits challenge the law, citing concerns over trademark use and political overreach.

Palm Beach County officials: The county emphasizes the change is 'branding only,' with operations unaffected. A licensing agreement prohibits financial benefits to Trump and ensures free use of the name, though some commissioners opposed the measure in a 4-3 vote.

Meta's AI image generator sparks privacy backlash over Instagram photo usage

Meta's new Muse Image AI tool lets users generate images using public Instagram photos without notifying the original posters, raising concerns about consent and misuse. Users can opt out via Instagram settings, but the feature is enabled by default for public accounts. This comes amid ongoing scrutiny of Meta's privacy practices and growing public skepticism about AI.

Meta's Muse Image tool, launched this week, allows users to create AI-generated images by tagging public Instagram profiles, effectively turning users' photos into raw material for AI creations without their knowledge. While private accounts and minors are protected by default, adult users with public profiles must manually disable the feature in Instagram's 'Sharing and reuse' settings.

The feature has drawn criticism from privacy advocates who argue it enables potential misuse, including harassment and non-consensual image manipulation. techcrunch.com reports this fits a pattern of Meta broadly using people's data unless they actively opt out, recalling past controversies like the Cambridge Analytica scandal that resulted in a $5 billion FTC fine.

Notably, users receive no notification when their images are used in AI generations, and any created images persist even if the original poster later changes their settings. tomsguide.com warns this creates a 'privacy landmine' where people's likenesses could be repurposed without recourse.

The launch comes as regulators globally increase scrutiny of AI-generated content. The UK's Ofcom is already investigating X (formerly Twitter) over similar concerns with its Grok AI tool, while bbc.co.uk notes Meta plans to expand Muse Image to Facebook and Messenger soon.

Meta defends the tool as offering 'advanced reasoning' for complex prompts and emphasizes user control through opt-out settings. However, critics argue the company should require explicit opt-in consent rather than placing the burden on users to protect their own privacy.

Oil prices swing as U.S.-Iran tensions threaten Hormuz shipping

Oil prices fell sharply Thursday after mediators worked to prevent U.S.-Iran hostilities from escalating, reversing Wednesday's surge when President Trump declared their ceasefire over. The volatility reflects market uncertainty about potential disruptions to the 20% of global oil supplies that transit the Strait of Hormuz.

Brent crude dropped 2.7% to $75.78 and WTI fell 2.6% to $71.57 as Qatar and Pakistan mediated between Washington and Tehran. This followed Wednesday's 4-5% price spikes when Trump threatened to reimpose a naval blockade after Iranian attacks on tankers in the Hormuz choke point.

The Strait of Hormuz remains the flashpoint, with tanker traffic slowing but continuing despite Iranian missile strikes on U.S. assets in four Gulf states and U.S. retaliatory bombings of 90 Iranian military targets. Analysts note the market is pricing in intermittent 'missile skirmishes' rather than a full closure of the waterway.

Goldman Sachs projects two scenarios: either flows normalize by late July if negotiations resume, or further disruptions occur if talks collapse. The U.S. revocation of Iranian oil sanction waivers adds supply uncertainty, though Saudi price cuts for Asian buyers are absorbing some volatility.

Citi analysts predict a return to negotiations within weeks, noting both sides have economic incentives to avoid infrastructure destruction. Trump's focus on stable markets and Iran's reliance on Hormuz control as leverage suggest continued oscillation between conflict and diplomacy.

The security situation remains fragile, with some insurers advising ships to pause Hormuz transits. Prices may trade in a $75-85 range near-term, with upside risk if attacks escalate but limited by expectations of eventual de-escalation given both nations' aversion to full-scale war.

Rising leveraged ETFs raise stock market volatility concerns

Leveraged ETFs and record margin debt ($1.4 trillion) are amplifying market risks, with experts warning they could exacerbate swings in an already uncertain market. The surge in these high-risk products, particularly in tech and semiconductor sectors, mirrors patterns seen before past selloffs.

Leveraged ETFs and margin debt have reached unprecedented levels, with $220 billion now held in leveraged ETFs—nearly double since March—and margin loans hitting $1.4 trillion in May. These instruments magnify gains but also losses, as seen when a triple-leveraged semiconductor ETF dropped 31% in a day, triple its index's decline.

The mechanics of these products create self-reinforcing cycles: fund managers buy derivatives to maintain leverage, prompting banks to hedge by purchasing underlying stocks, which fuels rallies. Conversely, downturns force rapid unwinding, accelerating selloffs. This dynamic recently triggered circuit breakers in South Korea’s semiconductor-heavy market.

Financing costs have spiked mid-year—unusually—due to demand for leveraged positions and IPOs. Analysts compare current conditions to late 2024, when similar leverage preceded volatility. Barclays warns unwinding large positions quickly could become 'the largest non-discretionary risk in the market.'

Despite low VIX levels, hedgers are bracing for shocks. The AI-driven rally masks fragility, as leveraged bets concentrate in tech stocks like Nvidia and Tesla. Direxion’s triple-leveraged semiconductor ETF surged 700% with Micron but risks near-total wipeout on a 30% drop.

Experts caution that while demand persists during the bull market, leverage could turn minor corrections into crises. 'The more money that buys and sells mechanically,' notes ETF.com’s Dave Nadig, 'the more volatility will inevitably expand.'
