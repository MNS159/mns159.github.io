Khamenei’s Funeral Culminates Amid Strikes, Regional Uncertainty

Iran is burying Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in Mashhad on Thursday, concluding six days of mass funerals that drew millions in Iran and Iraq. The burial comes as the U.S. launched new strikes on Iranian positions, fraying a fragile ceasefire and deepening regional uncertainty.

Iran is in the final stage of an unprecedented funeral for Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, the supreme leader killed by an Israeli strike on February 28. After processions in Tehran, Qom, and the Iraqi holy cities of Najaf and Karbala, his body arrived in Mashhad for burial at the Shrine of Imam Reza, Iran’s most sacred site. He will be interred alongside four family members killed in the same strike.

Mourners, estimated by local officials to number 15 million in Mashhad alone, have chanted ‘Kill Trump’ and waved placards demanding vengeance. The funeral prayer was led by 101-year-old ayatollah Hossein Noori Hamedani. Water sprinklers were deployed as temperatures hit 95°F.

Notably absent from all ceremonies is Mojtaba Khamenei, the new supreme leader and son of the slain ayatollah. He has not appeared publicly since inheriting the role, and reports indicate he was wounded in the same attack that killed his father. His absence has drawn intense scrutiny as Iran’s leadership transitions under the shadow of war.

The funeral coincides with a breakdown in the ceasefire between the U.S. and Iran. The U.S. launched new strikes targeting Iranian naval capabilities after Tehran attacked three ships in the Strait of Hormuz. President Trump warned of ‘much worse’ consequences. Iran responded by striking U.S. bases in Bahrain and Kuwait. The dual drama of mass mourning and renewed military strikes underscores the region’s volatility.

Khamenei’s rule spanned more than three decades, during which he consolidated power, marginalized elected institutions, and deepened the influence of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps. The war has strained Iran’s economy and triggered a crackdown on protests, but the turnout at his funeral signals continued support for the regime among large segments of the population.

White House launches 25 'Freedom Fuel' stations selling gas at $3.47 a gallon in Pennsylvania and New Jersey

On July 7, 2026, the White House announced the launch of 25 Freedom Fuel gas stations in Pennsylvania and New Jersey selling gasoline at $3.47 per gallon, below the national average of $3.79. Administration officials stated the federal government has no role in the network and is not subsidizing the fuel. The company behind the stations, Freedom Fuel Network, LLC, is private; trademark filings and location records show at least eight former Sunoco stations are among the sites.

Implications

The initiative could intensify political debate over gasoline prices ahead of the November 2026 midterm elections. Questions about the network's ownership, supply chain, and pricing sustainability remain unanswered, potentially inviting regulatory or congressional scrutiny. The ability of the stations to maintain below-market prices without subsidy — as the White House claims — will determine whether the move is seen as a durable policy achievement or a short-term political gimmick.

How different sides see it

White House / Trump Administration: The administration argues the initiative is a direct fulfillment of President Trump's promise to lower gas prices, with a private retailer voluntarily reducing profit margins to offer $3.47 fuel. Officials say the government has no financial involvement and no outside entity is subsidizing the discounts, framing this as market-driven relief for consumers ahead of America's 250th birthday.

Democrats: Democrats are expected to question the transparency of the Freedom Fuel Network, noting the White House has not identified the retailer, the fuel supplier, or how the discounts are funded. They likely argue this is a political stunt that evades responsibility for broader energy policy, and that without full disclosure, consumers and regulators cannot verify claims of no subsidy.

Republicans: Republicans generally support the initiative as evidence of President Trump's commitment to lower energy costs, praising the private sector's role without government mandates. Some may caution that the stations' long-term viability and lack of public ownership details could raise accountability concerns, but most prioritize the immediate relief for voters in key battleground states like Pennsylvania.

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman told CNBC that GPT-5.6 Sol is 54% more token efficient on agentic coding tasks and matches or beats competitors. The model is rolling out to a small group of trusted partners at the U.S. government's request. This efficiency gain directly addresses enterprise cost concerns, and the government collaboration signals a new level of oversight for frontier AI systems.

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman announced Thursday that GPT-5.6 Sol, the company's latest model, is 54% more token efficient on agentic coding tasks, a key metric for enterprise AI spending. Altman said the model is "as good or better" than competing models, emphasizing that every enterprise is now focused on the value they get for their AI spend. The announcement came as OpenAI begins rolling out GPT-5.6 Sol, along with Terra and Luna variants, to a small group of trusted partners. Altman noted that the company limited the launch at the request of the U.S. government, and that he worked personally with Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, and National Cyber Director Sean Cairncross on the approval process. He described the collaboration as a "collaborative back and forth" where the government raised issues for OpenAI to address before granting broader access.

The GPT-5.6 series represents the latest in a rapid cadence of model releases from OpenAI. Earlier this month, the company rolled out GPT-5.5 Instant and GPT-5.5 Instant Mini, which improved accuracy, conciseness, and personalization while reducing overformatting and factual issues [help.openai.com](https://help.openai.com/en/articles/6825453-chatgpt-release-notes?q=old+version). This sets a precedent for federal oversight of frontier AI, potentially influencing how other companies launch their most advanced systems. US Home Prices Hit All-Time High as Sales Slow and Mortgage Rates Rise

Existing-home sales fell 2.4% in June to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 4.09 million units, while the median sale price rose to a record $440,600, up 1.8% year-over-year. Mortgage rates in the mid-6% range and lean inventory continue to suppress activity, particularly for lower-priced homes.

Sales of previously owned homes declined 2.4% month-over-month in June to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 4.09 million, missing analysts’ expectations of a slight gain, according to the National Association of Realtors (NAR). Year-over-year sales were 2.8% higher. The dip reflects closed contracts signed in May, when the average 30-year fixed mortgage rate was still climbing after surging in March at the onset of the Iran war. NAR Chief Economist Lawrence Yun noted that homebuyers remain highly sensitive to affordability conditions, though job growth—more than half a million added since January—is providing a cushion.

The median existing-home price hit an all-time high of $440,600, a 1.8% increase from June 2025. Inventory edged down 0.6% from May to 1.56 million units, a 4.6-month supply—still below the 6-month threshold considered balanced. Yun cautioned that without consistent inventory gains, price acceleration could worsen affordability. Sales on the high end continued to outperform: homes priced between $750,000 and $1 million saw a 14% year-over-year increase, and those above $1 million rose 18%, while sales of homes under $100,000 fell 1.7%. All-cash sales represented 25% of transactions, down from 29% a year ago, while first-time buyers made up 33%, up from 30%.

Regionally, sales declined month-over-month in all areas except the Northeast. The report aligns with recent Redfin data showing the median home-sale price crossing $400,000 for the first time in the four weeks ending June 7, with monthly payments nearing $2,619. Despite elevated prices, pending sales have fallen for four consecutive weeks, driven by economic uncertainty tied to the Iran conflict and inflation concerns, though markets like Nashville and Austin are seeing more negotiable terms for buyers.

Auxilium Biotechnologies Begins Human Trial for Nerve Implant First Bioprinted in Space

Auxilium Biotechnologies has launched the first clinical trial for a nerve-regeneration implant, the NeuroSpan Bridge, which was originally bioprinted on the International Space Station using its AMP-1 bioprinter. The Neurospan-1 trial will enroll 80 patients with traumatic nerve injuries across multiple US medical centers. This marks the first time a space-manufactured medical device has entered human testing, highlighting the potential of microgravity for producing advanced implants.

Auxilium Biotechnologies announced the start of a clinical trial for its NeuroSpan Bridge, a nerve-regeneration implant that was first bioprinted in microgravity aboard the International Space Station. The implant, created using the company’s AMP-1 (Auxilium Microfabrication Platform) bioprinter, is designed to guide nerve regeneration after traumatic injuries such as those from car accidents or workplace incidents. The trial, named Neurospan-1, is being conducted at New York University Langone, UCSF, Ohio State University Wexner Medical Center, and the San Antonio Orthopaedic Group, and will assess safety, efficacy, and comparison to current treatments.

Prior to the trial, Auxilium achieved a major milestone in February 2025 by successfully printing eight implantable medical devices for peripheral nerve repair aboard the ISS. The AMP-1 system, which requires less than a minute of astronaut time per print session, uses lightweight cartridges preloaded with biological materials. Microgravity enables uniform material distribution and the creation of fine, intricate structures—such as the microchannels in the NeuroSpan Bridge—that would collapse under Earth’s gravity. The company has also demonstrated the ability to bioprint perfusable vasculature, further showcasing the platform’s capabilities.

The trial represents a significant step for space-based manufacturing and regenerative medicine. By leveraging funding from NASA’s InSpace Production Applications (InSpa) program, Auxilium has positioned itself as a leader in space biomanufacturing. The company also emphasizes sustainability: leftover metal powder from the printing process is recycled by partner Global Advanced Metals, creating a closed-loop system. Longer-term, the technology could support medical care on deep-space missions to the Moon and Mars.
