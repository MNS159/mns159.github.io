U.S. strikes near Iranian nuclear plant escalate conflict

U.S. airstrikes hit areas near Iran's Bushehr nuclear power plant and civilian infrastructure in Bushehr province, with Iran reporting 14 deaths over two days of attacks. The escalation threatens to collapse a fragile ceasefire and disrupt global oil shipments through the Strait of Hormuz.

The U.S. military confirmed strikes on 90 targets across Iran, including air defenses and naval infrastructure, while Iranian officials reported damage to civilian areas near the Bushehr nuclear plant and fishing piers. The Pentagon stated the operations aimed to degrade Iran's ability to threaten shipping lanes, following Iranian attacks on tankers in the Strait of Hormuz earlier this week.

This marks the most significant military exchange since February, when U.S. and Israeli strikes initiated the conflict. While traffic through the Strait had partially recovered after a tentative June deal, Thursday's strikes saw only six tankers transit the waterway—a fraction of pre-war levels. Analysts warn renewed fighting could spike global energy prices.

The timing coincides with the burial of former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, killed in the war's opening strikes. Hardliners in Iran appear to be using the funeral period to demonstrate military resolve, while pragmatists push for sanctions relief through negotiations. President Trump's mixed signals—approving strikes while claiming talks continue—add to the uncertainty.

Regional U.S. allies faced retaliatory attacks, with Kuwait intercepting missiles and drones. The strikes near Bushehr raise particular concern as the plant houses Iran's only operational nuclear reactor. While no radiation leaks were reported, previous strikes in the area had avoided direct hits on nuclear facilities.

With midterm elections approaching, the administration faces pressure to stabilize oil markets. However, Trump's threat of 'much worse' strikes if Iran continues ship attacks suggests further volatility ahead. Mediators scramble to salvage negotiations before the conflict fully reignites.

Court clears Le Pen's path to France's 2027 presidency amid legal challenges

A French appeals court upheld Marine Le Pen's embezzlement conviction but reduced her ban from public office, allowing her to run in the 2027 presidential election while serving house arrest with an electronic monitor. This positions her as a leading contender despite legal uncertainties, with her far-right National Rally party currently polling ahead of all others.

The French appeals court delivered a nuanced verdict on Marine Le Pen's embezzlement case, upholding her conviction for misusing European Parliament funds but reducing her ban on seeking public office from five years to 45 months—30 of which are suspended. With 15 months already served, Le Pen is now eligible to run in the 2027 presidential election. However, she faces a year of house arrest with an electronic monitor, which she plans to challenge at France's highest court, the Court of Cassation.

Le Pen's candidacy introduces significant uncertainty into the 2027 race. Her National Rally (RN) party, which opposes globalization, immigration, and stringent ecological policies, has seen its popularity surge in recent years. Before her conviction, polls suggested Le Pen could secure up to 37% of the vote in the first round, a notable increase from her 2022 performance. However, her legal troubles could complicate her campaign, especially if the Court of Cassation issues a final ruling close to or during the election.

The court's decision also has implications for the RN's internal dynamics. Speculation had grown that Le Pen would step aside for her protege, Jordan Bardella, the party's 30-year-old president, who appeals to a younger demographic. Bardella, seen as more moderate and pro-business than Le Pen, could still play a key role if Le Pen succeeds, potentially as her prime minister.

Le Pen's ability to navigate her legal challenges while maintaining her political momentum will be critical. Her supporters may view her conviction as evidence of systemic persecution, reinforcing her anti-establishment appeal. However, broader electoral success will require winning over voters beyond her core base, a task complicated by her criminal status and the historical tendency of French voters to unite against far-right candidates in the final round.

The 2027 election could mark a pivotal moment in French politics, with Le Pen potentially facing a fragmented opposition struggling to coalesce behind a credible centrist candidate. Her campaign, conducted partly under house arrest with an electronic monitor, would be unprecedented in French political history, testing both legal norms and public tolerance for convicted politicians seeking the nation's highest office.

White House North Portico columns undergo restoration work

The White House confirmed that the North Portico columns are undergoing standard restoration work for stone repairs. Construction crews have been photographed performing maintenance, including stripping layers of old paint from the 1830-built columns. Scaffolding and semi-transparent coverings are currently in place during the work.

Implications

The restoration could extend the lifespan of the historic columns but may spark debates about preserving architectural integrity versus modernization. The project follows other White House renovations under Trump, potentially setting a precedent for future executive mansion updates. Costs and exact scope remain undisclosed, leaving room for scrutiny over taxpayer-funded improvements.

How different sides see it

Administration/Trump allies: The administration argues this is necessary preservation work, with Interior Secretary Burgum stating they're restoring historic plasterwork from base to crown. Trump has emphasized the need to address decades of paint buildup, framing it as preventative maintenance to avoid further deterioration.

Historic preservation advocates: Some architects and preservationists express concern about potential design changes, particularly after Commission of Fine Arts chairman Cook suggested replacing Ionic columns with more ornate Corinthian styles. They argue this could alter the building's historic character, as the porticos have remained unchanged since their 1820s construction.

Fed names Andreessen, McMillon to key policy task forces

Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh announced the members of five new policy task forces, including venture capitalist Marc Andreessen and former Walmart CEO Doug McMillon. The groups will examine Fed operations across communications, data, balance sheet management, and inflation frameworks, with recommendations expected by year-end.

The Federal Reserve has tapped an unusually diverse group of outsiders for its new policy task forces, including tech investor Marc Andreessen, former Walmart CEO Doug McMillon, and former Bank of England Governor Mervyn King. This marks Chairman Kevin Warsh's first major structural change since taking office, implementing his vision of bringing fresh perspectives to central banking.

The five task forces will focus on: 1) Fed communications 2) balance sheet management 3) economic data utilization 4) productivity/jobs impact 5) inflation frameworks. As noted in Warsh's June press conference, each group will 'start with first principles' and consider alternative approaches to current practices.

Notably, nearly half the appointees come from outside economics - including business leaders and technologists. As tradevae.com reported, Warsh deliberately sought international perspectives, arguing outsiders can 'see things more clearly.' The inclusion of Andreessen suggests particular focus on AI's economic impacts.

The move accelerates Warsh's push for institutional reform. As American Banker observed, this 'Warsh era' initiative aims to make the Fed 'fit for purpose' amid technological disruption. The task forces will operate independently but must align recommendations with the FOMC.

With all groups expected to conclude by December, the Fed could implement major policy changes before 2027. Key areas under review include the 2% inflation target (which Warsh has questioned) and the Fed's economic projections methodology - signaling potential departures from long-standing norms.

Biotech ETFs surge as sector outpaces broader market

Biotech-focused ETFs like Invesco's IBBQ and State Street's XPH have gained 57-64% over the past year, outpacing the S&P 500, as M&A activity and drug development progress fuel sector optimism. The rally comes despite higher volatility and regulatory risks inherent in biotech investing.

The biotech sector is experiencing a strong rebound, with the SPDR S&P Biotech ETF (XBI) up 89% over the past year and 17% in just the past month. This outperformance comes despite the sector's traditional volatility and comes without exposure to AI megacaps like NVIDIA that have driven other market rallies.

Two competing ETFs illustrate different approaches to biotech exposure: Invesco's Nasdaq Biotechnology ETF (IBBQ) offers broad diversification across 253 biotech and pharma companies with a 0.19% expense ratio, while State Street's XPH provides more concentrated, equal-weighted exposure to 65 pharmaceutical firms at 0.35% expense. IBBQ's broader diversification and lower cost make it appealing for general exposure, while XPH offers purer pharma plays.

Recent sector strength appears driven by fundamentals rather than speculation, with increased M&A activity and several late-stage drug candidates approaching commercialization. Obesity treatments, rare disease therapies, and oncology drugs are particularly active areas. However, the sector remains risky - both funds have experienced maximum drawdowns exceeding 30% in recent years.

Individual biotech firms are also making waves, with VERAXA Biotech advancing its T-cell engager therapy and some stocks tripling on clinical trial news. However, setbacks like Roche ending two Huntington's disease programs remind investors of the sector's binary outcomes.

Analysts note this rally differs from past biotech booms by featuring more advanced-stage candidates rather than early science projects. While the momentum is strong, the sector's history of boom-bust cycles suggests cautious optimism may be warranted despite current euphoria.
