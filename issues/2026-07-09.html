US Moves to Delist Syria as a State Sponsor of Terrorism

The United States announced it will remove Syria's designation as a 'state sponsor of terrorism,' a label in place since 1979. This decision aims to promote investment and economic support for Syria under the new leadership of President Ahmed al-Sharaa.

The delisting decision, confirmed by Secretary of State Marco Rubio, is a significant shift in U.S. foreign policy and reflects a vote of confidence in Syria's interim government led by Ahmed al-Sharaa. The move will take effect in 45 days unless blocked by Congress. By removing Syria from the terrorism list, Washington aims to facilitate economic development in the war-torn country and allow it to re-engage with the global financial system.

Syria has been under the terrorism designation since 1979, which has hindered international investment and reconstruction efforts following years of brutal civil war. The U.S. had long cited Syria's support for groups such as Hezbollah and its historic ties to Iran as reasons for this designation. As Secretary Rubio noted, lifting these sanctions is expected to unlock international trade opportunities and help rebuild a unified Syria, benefiting the region and beyond.

President Trump met with al-Sharaa during a NATO summit in Turkey to discuss these changes. His support for al-Sharaa comes amid skepticism from Israel, which has conducted numerous airstrikes against perceived threats in Syria. Al-Sharaa’s government has promised not to support terrorism, providing formal assurances as part of the delisting process.

If Syria is removed from the list, it will leave only three countries—Iran, North Korea, and Cuba—on the U.S. terrorism blacklist. The move to delist Syria indicates a broader strategic shift in the U.S. approach to the region, potentially paving the way for new diplomatic engagements and economic partnerships with the newly established Syrian leadership.

US Senate Urged to Examine AI Impacts on Labor and Economy

A bipartisan group of U.S. senators is pressing the Labor Department to collect comprehensive data regarding the effects of artificial intelligence on employment. This initiative aims to provide transparency and inform policymakers and the workforce regarding AI's implications in an evolving labor market.

Senators Mark Warner (D-Va.) and Ted Budd (R-N.C.) have introduced the Workforce Transparency Act, calling for the Department of Labor (DOL), along with the Bureau of Labor Statistics and the Census Bureau, to create a public database aimed at tracking AI's impact on the workforce. This bill would require aggregated workforce data to be shared with the public, enabling better decision-making in light of technological changes.

The senators highlight the necessity of accurate data as AI technologies continue to permeate various industries, and express concern about its foreseeable consequences on job displacement and creation. They emphasize that timely data will help stakeholders navigate this transformation, ensuring that both businesses and workers are prepared for the challenges posed by these advancements.

In addition to this initiative, the AI-Related Job Impacts Clarity Act has also been introduced, compelling companies to report quarterly on AI-related employment changes. These reports will include metrics on job cuts, new hires, and shifts in workforce dynamics attributable to AI technologies.

This legislative movement is backed by significant industry players, including Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI, reflecting a growing consensus on the need for transparency regarding AI's role in shaping the job market. The DOL's response to these proposals will be critical in shaping how the U.S. labor market adapts to ongoing technological changes.

Truecaller Challenges India's Telecom Regulator Over Spam Rules

Truecaller has publicly challenged the Telecom Regulatory Authority of India (TRAI) over its regulatory framework restricting spam alerts for caller ID apps. This dispute highlights concerns that the rules are enabling spam calls and eroding consumer trust in legitimate communications.

On July 8, 2026, Truecaller CEO Rishit Jhunjhunwala contested TRAI's approach to regulating caller ID apps, especially regarding the mandatory whitelisting of calls from designated 1400 and 1600 number series. According to Jhunjhunwala, this regulation has led to an increase in spam calls, undermining user confidence in legitimate business communications. Reports indicate that Truecaller users have been ignoring a significant majority of calls from these series, with 81% of 1400 series calls and 79% of 1600 series calls going unanswered in recent months.

The issues stem from a regulatory framework established in 2024, aimed at curbing spam and scam calls in India’s massive telecom market. TRAI designated specific number series for commercial use, intending to help consumers identify legitimate communications. However, Truecaller claims this framework has inadvertently made it more challenging to distinguish between legitimate and spam calls.

In response to the regulations, Truecaller has implemented a 'Frequently Blocked' badge to inform users about numbers that have been reported as unwanted by other users. This measure allows Truecaller to provide some level of protection against potential spam without directly labeling the designated calls as spam, which the regulations prohibit.

The friction between Truecaller and TRAI is occurring at a critical juncture for the app, given that India accounts for over 350 million of its 500 million active users. Jhunjhunwala has publicly stated that Truecaller will provide data to the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology to support any regulatory decisions, emphasizing that enforcement should be directed at genuine spam perpetrators rather than companies like Truecaller that aim to protect consumers.

This performance reinforces Levi Strauss's competitive positioning in the retail market, particularly as consumers continue to respond favorably to its diverse product range even amid fluctuating economic conditions. China's Inflation Struggles Amid Economic Growth Efforts

In June, China's consumer prices rose only 1%, falling short of expectations, while wholesale prices increased 4.1%, marking the fastest growth since July 2022. This divergence signals ongoing economic challenges, particularly in domestic demand amidst rising energy costs.

According to data from the National Bureau of Statistics, China's consumer price index (CPI) growth of 1% in June lagged behind economists' forecasts of 1.1%, a slight dip from May's 1.2%. Core CPI growth similarly decreased to 1%, demonstrating a stagnation in consumer spending and confidence. In contrast, China's producer price index (PPI) rose by 4.1%, indicating substantial cost pressures faced by manufacturers, linked to higher energy prices and supply disruptions from broader geopolitical tensions, particularly in the Middle East.

The rising PPI underscores the changing dynamics in China's economy, as energy costs and a surge in demand for artificial intelligence and technology-related products continue to influence prices. The recent growth in export and manufacturing sectors, particularly driven by technological demand, contrasts sharply with weak domestic consumption and a faltering housing market, a dynamic recognized by many analysts as a defining characteristic of China's economic landscape.

Despite the mixed economic signals, the Chinese government appears hesitant to implement new stimulus measures while export-led growth remains robust. The upcoming Politburo meeting at the end of July could prompt more decisive economic policies if the slowdown in consumer demand continues. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) recently adjusted their growth forecast for China to 4.6% for the year, reflecting expectations of resilience in manufacturing and exports.

The tensions between export-driven growth and domestic weakness raise concerns for future economic stability, as household spending remains depressed. Policymakers face the challenge of balancing immediate economic support with longer-term structural reforms to bolster consumer confidence and spending, especially in light of ongoing external challenges.

Scientists Identify Gene That May Slow Early Menopause and Infertility

A recent study has identified genetic signals linked to menopause timing, highlighting potential avenues to predict and possibly delay early menopause. This discovery could improve reproductive health for women and inform fertility treatments.

In a significant development, researchers have pinpointed 290 genetic variants associated with the age at which women experience menopause, as documented in a study published in *Nature*. Many of these genes are involved in DNA repair processes, suggesting that understanding these mechanisms could lead to new strategies for addressing early menopause and infertility. This information not only enhances the knowledge of factors that influence menopause timing but also opens possibilities for therapies that could extend fertility.

Currently, women typically begin menopause around the age of 51, but the onset varies considerably based on genetics and environmental influences. The findings from this study underscore the importance of genetic factors that have been less understood compared to environmental risks like smoking or chemotherapy. Researchers are now working to improve the prediction of menopause age by incorporating various factors, which could guide women in their reproductive choices and health management.

While the study offers promising insights, experts caution that genetic manipulation carries risks, including potential links to cancer when dealing with DNA-repair genes. Therefore, researchers are cautious about how these findings are applied in therapeutic contexts.

As research continues, the implications for women's health could be significant. By identifying women at risk for premature menopause, interventions could be tailored more effectively to enhance fertility and overall reproductive health. The researchers aim to replicate these findings in diverse populations to broaden the applicability and effectiveness of their genetic insights.

EU Biotech Act Seeks to Strengthen Patent Incentives for Innovators

The EU is proposing the Biotech Act, which includes a contentious measure to extend patent protections for biotech medicines by 12 months. This move aims to incentivize innovation and investment in the biotech sector, though it has faced significant criticism for potentially raising healthcare costs and delaying patient access to treatments.

The proposed Biotech Act introduces a 12-month extension for supplementary protection certificates (SPCs) for biotech medicines. Proponents argue that this extension could foster greater innovation by making Europe a more attractive place for biotech investment. An analysis by the European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations suggests that stronger patent protections could lead to increased research spending and foreign investments in the sector.

However, not everyone is convinced. Critics, including members from the environment and legal affairs committees of the European Parliament, argue that longer monopolies may delay patient access to affordable medicines and hinder competition. The environment committee, for example, emphasizes that increased market protections could lead to higher healthcare costs without a clear link to innovation.

Debate over the Commission’s proposal has been intense, with some lawmakers stating that extended monopolies do not guarantee greater innovation. They call for more robust evidence to support claims that SPC extensions would benefit patients or the healthcare system in general.

The opposition is noteworthy, as key committees within the Parliament have moved to scrap the extension of patent protections. This split within the EU showcases the tension between fostering innovation and ensuring patient access to medicines, highlighting the complexities of advancing biotech policies in a diverse regulatory landscape.
