Houthis impose naval blockade on Saudi Arabia, risking 7% of global oil supply

Yemen's Iran-aligned Houthi rebels declared an immediate naval blockade against Saudi Arabia, closing the Bab el-Mandeb strait to Saudi vessels. The choke point handles 7.4 million barrels per day of oil shipments, representing 7% of global supply. This escalates regional tensions and threatens energy markets already strained by Gulf conflicts.

The blockade retaliates against what Houthis call Saudi Arabia's 12-year siege of Yemen, including port and airport closures. It follows recent missile exchanges, including Houthi strikes on Abha airport after Saudi-backed forces bombed Sanaa's airport to block an Iranian plane.

Saudi Arabia had already diverted 70% of its crude exports to the Red Sea port of Yanbu after the Strait of Hormuz became impassable due to U.S.-Iran tensions. Yanbu shipments surged from 973,000 to 4 million barrels per day this year, all now vulnerable to the Bab el-Mandeb closure.

The 20-mile-wide strait connects the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden, forming part of the Suez Canal route. Even without physical attacks, the threat alone could reroute ships around Africa as occurred during previous Houthi Red Sea campaigns, adding weeks to voyages.

Global oil markets face compounded disruptions: the Gulf conflict already cut 10% of supply, while Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb together normally handle 25% of seaborne oil. The Houthis acted after Iran urged the strait's closure if U.S. strikes on Iranian infrastructure continued.

The Petroline pipeline to Yanbu, built in the 1980s as an alternative to Hormuz, may now prove inadequate. Its 7 million barrel/day capacity is already strained by the export surge, leaving Saudi Arabia with few remaining options to bypass both blocked chokepoints.

Trump's AI standards chief resigns amid debate over Chinese model restrictions

Chris Fall resigned as director of the Commerce Department's Center for AI Standards and Innovation after just three months, leaving the key federal AI office without permanent leadership. The departure comes as the Trump administration debates whether to impose sweeping restrictions on advanced Chinese AI models like Moonshot's Kimi K3.

The resignation coincides with revived internal discussions about adding Chinese AI labs to the Commerce Department's Entity List, which would cut off U.S. access without licenses. Previous efforts to restrict Chinese models were blocked last year by officials opposing heavy-handed regulation, but national security hawks have gained influence recently.

CAISI has completed over 40 evaluations of frontier AI models but lacks enforcement power—its findings don't bind companies or mandate testing. When Commerce blocked Anthropic's models earlier this summer, the decision came from political appointees rather than CAISI's technical staff.

Chinese open-source models now handle about 30% of workloads for some U.S. intermediaries like OpenRouter, raising concerns that prompt data could strengthen China's AI development. Unlike chip export controls, this 'data exfiltration' problem creates a compounding advantage as Chinese models learn from American usage patterns.

NIST Director Arvind Raman will serve as acting CAISI director during the leadership vacuum. Raman oversees NIST's broader standards work, including for emerging technologies, but lacks Fall's specific AI safety expertise from his prior roles at DOE and MITRE.

The next director will either oversee expanded authority if Chinese model restrictions proceed or preside over an increasingly marginal agency if they don't. Potential restrictions could reshape the open-source ecosystem by eliminating cost-effective Chinese alternatives, benefiting closed U.S. labs like OpenAI and Anthropic.

China's open-source AI models challenge US dominance, sparking policy debates

Chinese tech firms are releasing powerful AI models with open-weight architectures, allowing free access and modification — a sharp contrast to US companies' closed systems. This approach is gaining global traction but faces potential restrictions as Beijing considers limiting overseas access to domestic models.

Open-weight models like Alibaba's Qwen and Moonshot's Kimi enable developers to bypass API fees and hosting costs by running AI locally, dramatically reducing operational expenses compared to closed systems like OpenAI's GPT-4. This has led to rapid adoption in emerging markets where cloud computing costs are prohibitive.

The strategy mirrors China's playbook in 5G and semiconductor sectors — using open architectures to build global developer ecosystems. However, recent signals from Beijing suggest concerns about technology leakage, with policymakers debating export controls similar to those on chip-making equipment.

US AI labs face mounting pressure to justify subscription-based models as open alternatives proliferate. Anthropic recently reversed its closed-model policy after losing developers to open-source alternatives, highlighting the competitive pressure.

Security researchers warn that unfettered access to powerful models could accelerate disinformation campaigns and cyberattacks. Unlike closed systems with usage monitoring, open-weight models allow anonymous, offline deployment that evades content moderation.

Judge pauses Paramount-Warner Bros. merger amid antitrust concerns

A federal judge issued a 14-day temporary restraining order halting Paramount's $110 billion acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery, responding to a lawsuit from 12 state attorneys general who argue the deal would violate antitrust laws by creating a media giant controlling nearly one-third of films and basic cable programming. The order preserves the status quo while courts assess whether the merger would harm competition, though Paramount maintains the deal is lawful and pro-competitive, with plans to close by September.

The lawsuit targets the merger under the Clayton Antitrust Act, arguing it would consolidate control over wide-release theatrical distribution—a market where the combined entity would hold substantial share. Paramount had offered to delay closing until mid-August during Friday's hearing, but the judge's order now forces at least a two-week pause. If the states secure a preliminary injunction after this period, the delay could stretch into 2027, mirroring the timeline of a similar case involving Nexstar and Tegna. Paramount faces financial penalties if the deal stalls past September 30, including a $650 million quarterly 'ticking fee' to Warner Bros. shareholders and a potential $7 billion breakup fee if regulators ultimately block it. While the DOJ and international regulators have approved the merger, the state coalition—led by California AG Rob Bonta—contends it would reduce competition in film and cable markets, raising prices and limiting content choices.

AMD launches Helios AI system, challenging Nvidia's dominance

AMD is shipping its first rack-scale AI system, Helios, to customers including Microsoft, marking its first real competition to Nvidia's Grace Blackwell and Vera Rubin systems. The move comes as tech giants scramble for AI compute capacity, with AMD shares rising 4% on the news. This could reshape the $100B+ AI chip market where Nvidia currently holds 95% share.

Helios combines AMD's GPUs, CPUs, networking chips and software in a single system, weighing up to 7,000 pounds. Each rack contains 18 compute trays with four Instinct MI400 GPUs and one EPYC CPU per tray, leveraging AMD's 2022 acquisition of networking firm Pensando. The system targets 'lowest cost per token' for AI inference workloads.

Microsoft's adoption signals a strategic shift, as it will use Helios alongside its custom Maia chips and Nvidia hardware. The company joins Meta, OpenAI and Oracle in committing to AMD's system, with Meta planning to deploy up to 6 gigawatts of AMD GPUs starting this year.

AMD's data center revenue grew 57% YoY in Q1 2026, with executives projecting tens of billions in AI revenue from 2027 onward. Analysts suggest Helios could help AMD capture 20-25% of the AI accelerator market, though it currently holds just 4.5% share compared to Nvidia's dominance.

The launch continues AMD's decade-long turnaround under CEO Lisa Su, who stabilized the company's roadmap after its near-collapse in the 2010s. EPYC server CPUs, first launched in 2017, rebuilt AMD's data center credibility and now power the compute backbone of Helios.

Costa Rican scientists cut microalgae farming costs by 95%

A team led by Costa Rican biologist Maritza Guerrero has developed a natural biostimulant, Phyco-Plus, using microalgae that enhances crop productivity, regenerates soils, and captures carbon dioxide. By reducing production costs by 95%, this innovation makes microalgae-based products affordable for local farmers, potentially transforming sustainable agriculture practices globally. This breakthrough positions Costa Rica as a leader in nature-based solutions for agricultural and environmental challenges.

The Phyco-Plus project, supported by the Inter-American Development Bank with a $200,000 grant, was developed at the Tecnológico de Costa Rica (TEC). Guerrero and her team cultivated microalgae in large ponds in Barva de Heredia, capable of holding up to 40,000 liters. The biostimulant is produced by centrifuging and harvesting the microalgae, which are then bottled for field application. This process not only regenerates soil microbiota but also enhances plant carbohydrate and phytohormone production, helping plants manage abiotic stress.

Microalgae are unicellular aquatic microorganisms that perform photosynthesis and produce valuable biomolecules such as proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, vitamins, and pigments. Their properties make them powerful agents for sustainable farming, contributing to oxygen production, carbon sequestration, and the stimulation of plant growth regulators.

Guerrero’s innovation aims to shift farmers’ mindsets towards using microorganism-based products. 'Microalgae could boost agriculture and transform farmers’ mindsets, encouraging them to use microorganism-based products. It’s about improving food quality, human quality, and water quality,' she explained. Farmers have already noted significant improvements in crop health and yield, with observations of taller plants and faster development in crops like potatoes and onions.
