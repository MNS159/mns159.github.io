Ferry carrying 133 people capsizes off Guyana, 67 rescued

The MV Barima ferry capsized near Guyana's Pomeroon River with 116 passengers and 17 crew aboard, leaving dozens missing. Sixty-seven people, including 15 children, have been rescued so far in an ongoing operation involving state and private vessels. The 1939-built vessel was equipped with 250 life jackets and multiple life rafts when it sank during its Georgetown-to-Port Kaituma route.

The aging MV Barima, measuring 40 meters long, had been in service for 87 years since its 1939 construction. Maritime experts note that vessels of this age typically face stricter safety inspections, raising questions about maintenance standards prior to the incident.

Rescue efforts face significant challenges due to the remote location near Iron Punt, where the vessel issued its distress call at 23:01 local time. The area's proximity to the North Atlantic coast exposes rescuers to strong currents and unpredictable weather conditions.

Guyana's government has mobilized a rare joint operation between its coastguard and private vessels, highlighting limited national maritime rescue capabilities. Medical teams are being deployed directly to the search area to provide immediate care to survivors pulled from the water.

The incident occurs amid growing regional concerns about aging ferry fleets in Caribbean nations. Similar accidents have occurred in recent years in Haiti and the Dominican Republic, where old vessels remain in service due to limited transportation alternatives.

Authorities have established assistance centers for families while withholding passenger names pending medical evaluations. This suggests possible injuries among survivors and reflects standard protocol for mass casualty incidents involving water immersion.

US and Iran escalate strikes after two US soldiers killed in Jordan

The US conducted its eighth consecutive night of strikes on Iranian targets near the Strait of Hormuz, including Qeshm Island, following an Iranian attack that killed two US soldiers in Jordan. Iran retaliated by targeting a Kuwaiti power plant for the second time in two days and closing the Strait of Hormuz to shipping. The exchange marks a dangerous escalation after the collapse of a preliminary ceasefire agreement.

The US strikes targeted Qeshm Island, a critical energy logistics hub at the mouth of the Strait of Hormuz, as well as the port city of Sirik and inland city of Hajjiabad. These locations are strategic for Iran's ability to project power and control shipping in the region. biz.chosun.com

Iran's response included missile launches toward Jordan, some of which were intercepted with assistance from Israeli missile defenses. The conflict has disrupted regional shipping, with Iran warning vessels influenced by the US would 'face accidents' in the Strait of Hormuz. This follows Tehran's declaration closing the strait, a move that could significantly impact global oil supplies if sustained.

Casualties continue to mount, with at least 50 Iranians killed in US strikes over three weeks according to Iranian officials. The US death toll now stands at 16 after a missing Navy pilot was declared dead. Both sides accuse each other of targeting civilian infrastructure.

Diplomatic efforts appear stalled, with Iran's Supreme Leader declaring US agreements 'worthless' after the collapse of the June ceasefire. The Gulf Cooperation Council has condemned Iran's attacks on Kuwaiti civilian infrastructure, signaling growing regional concern about escalation.

US Politics

Trump urges Congress to add Iran to Russia sanctions bill

President Donald Trump called on Republicans to include Iran in the Russia sanctions bill originally spearheaded by the late Senator Lindsey Graham. The revised bill, which has bipartisan support, imposes a tariff of up to 100% on the top five buyers of Russian oil and gas and grants the president waiver authority with Congressional justification. Trump emphasized that adding Iran was a priority for Graham and urged lawmakers to act swiftly.

Implications

Trump's push to include Iran could delay the bill's passage, as it may require reopening the legislative process. This could complicate efforts to undercut Russia's wartime revenues. Additionally, the inclusion of Iran could exacerbate tensions in the Middle East, especially given the recent collapse of the ceasefire between Iran and the U.S.

How different sides see it

Republicans: Republicans, led by President Trump, argue that adding Iran to the sanctions bill honors Lindsey Graham's legacy and strengthens U.S. efforts to counter Iran's influence. Trump has stated that this was Graham's intention and that it was 'going to happen.'

Democrats: Democrats, including Senator Richard Blumenthal, argue that the bill should proceed as it is, given its bipartisan support and the urgency of targeting Russian energy revenues. Blumenthal suggested that new Iran-related measures could be considered separately rather than reopening the current bill.

Blackstone Acquires Dresser Utility Solutions in Energy Transition Push

Blackstone Energy Transition Partners has agreed to acquire Dresser Utility Solutions from First Reserve, marking its first investment from its latest private equity energy transition fund. Dresser, a provider of infrastructure solutions for gas and water utilities, will gain access to Blackstone’s resources to expand its product portfolio and improve customer service. The deal underscores Blackstone’s focus on supporting utilities amid growing energy grid demands.

Blackstone’s acquisition of Dresser comes as utilities increasingly invest in modernizing aging infrastructure and adopting digital monitoring tools. Dresser, founded in 1880, specializes in metering technology, pressure control solutions, and infrastructure repair products, serving gas and water utilities globally. Its acquisition aligns with Blackstone’s broader strategy of investing in energy transition businesses, having deployed over $28 billion in equity globally across the sector.

First Reserve, the seller, originally acquired Dresser’s natural gas meters division from Baker Hughes in 2018 and rebranded it as Dresser Utility Solutions in 2021. The firm has a history of investing in energy infrastructure and equipment, typically deploying $50 million to $500 million per transaction. Dresser’s 2025 financials included $375 million in revenue and $75 million in EBITDA.

The transaction reflects broader market trends, with utilities seeking to optimize assets and reduce emissions. Strategic buyers like Badger Meter and Itron were previously seen as potential acquirers, though private equity interest prevailed. Blackstone plans to leverage Dresser’s established customer relationships and technological expertise to drive growth in the utility sector.

12 states sue to block $111B Paramount-Warner Bros merger

A coalition of 12 states led by California sued to block the Paramount-Warner Bros merger, arguing it would control 30% of blockbuster films and 27% of basic cable markets. The DOJ approved the deal, but states claim it harms theaters and cable providers by reducing competition.

The lawsuit hinges on a 1986 court precedent where AMC successfully argued that blockbuster films like 'E.T.' operate in a separate market from niche titles. States contend this logic still applies despite streaming's rise, as theaters rely on tentpole releases for revenue.

Paramount faces financial pressure to close the deal, with a $650M quarterly penalty if delayed past October. Meanwhile, theater chains warn merged studios could demand up to 60% of ticket revenue for hits, squeezing their ability to fund upgrades like premium seating.

The coalition exclusively comprises Democratic-led states, reflecting partisan divides in antitrust enforcement. This follows California's recent success in blocking the Nexstar-Tegna media merger despite federal approval.

Cable providers fear reduced bargaining power against a combined entity controlling CNN, HBO, and Paramount networks. The suit doesn't challenge streaming consolidation, focusing instead on theatrical and linear TV markets where the companies hold duopoly power.

Box office remains 16% below 2019 levels, making theaters vulnerable to distributor demands. Studios currently split ticket sales 50-50 for most films, but the merger could shift this balance further in their favor.
