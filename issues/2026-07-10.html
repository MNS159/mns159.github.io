Strait of Hormuz shipping plummets as US-Iran tensions flare

Ship traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has dropped sharply to just 23 vessels per day, down from a pre-conflict average of 138, following attacks by Iran on three tankers using a US-recommended route through Omani waters. The drop threatens global energy supplies, as the strait carries over 20% of the world's oil and gas.

The current conflict stems from competing claims over control of the strait. While the June 17 US-Iran memorandum of understanding committed Tehran to safe passage for 60 days, Iran continues to insist that ships must use its designated routes rather than the US-protected Omani path. This divergence has escalated tensions, with Iran attacking vessels using the Omani route and the US retaliating with strikes on Iranian military targets.

Historically, the strait has been open to free navigation under international law, but Iran has long sought to assert control and potentially charge transit fees. The recent decline in traffic follows a brief uptick after the June agreement, which temporarily eased economic sanctions on Iran and lifted a US naval blockade.

Maritime experts view the memorandum as ambiguous, particularly regarding Iran's rights over the strait's administration long-term. The International Maritime Risk Assessment Committee has maintained the strait's threat level at 'severe,' reflecting persistent risks of attacks and sea mines.

Oil and LNG tankers from Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and Liberia were among the vessels targeted in recent attacks, leading insurers to raise premiums and shipping firms to reconsider routes. The standoff continues despite Qatari mediation efforts to salvage the ceasefire.

Deadly wildfire in southern Spain kills 12, leaves 23 missing

A wildfire in Andalusia, Spain, has killed at least 12 people and left 23 missing, with most victims being foreign tourists. The blaze, one of Spain's deadliest on record, highlights the escalating wildfire risks across Europe amid rising temperatures.

The fire erupted near Los Gallardos in Almería, a rugged, tourist-heavy region, and spread rapidly due to dry conditions. Local officials described the devastation as unprecedented, with one mayor comparing the aftermath to a bomb site.

Many victims ignored evacuation orders or shelter-in-place directives, according to Andalusia's president. A survivor reportedly convinced others to stay indoors, a decision that may have contributed to the casualties.

Over 500 emergency workers are battling the flames as Europe enters what experts predict will be a severe wildfire season. Southern Spain's extreme heat and drought conditions mirror climate-driven patterns increasing fire risks globally.

The incident follows other recent extreme weather events worldwide, including Arizona's wildfires and Beijing's record rainfall, underscoring the interconnected nature of climate-related disasters across continents.

Telstra’s Massive Outage Linked to Outdated $22,000 Device

Telstra's recent mass outage, which disrupted emergency services for hundreds of Australians, was likely caused by a decade-old SyncServer S300 device that had reached its lifespan. The incident highlights systemic failures in Telstra's equipment lifecycle management and raises questions about cost-cutting practices.

The SyncServer S300, a device designed to synchronize network time, was central to Telstra’s outage. Manufactured until around 2016, the device’s firmware can only track up to 1,024 weeks, causing it to reset when it reaches this limit. This issue was reportedly known internally but deemed too complex to address promptly.

Industry insiders estimate replacing the device could have cost as little as $9,500, raising concerns over Telstra’s prioritization of cost efficiency over reliability. The telco’s CEO, Vicki Brady, apologized publicly but denied that job cuts or outsourcing contributed to the failure.

The Australian Communications and Media Authority is investigating Telstra’s compliance with legal obligations, which could result in civil penalties of up to $30 million. Meanwhile, the Communications Minister emphasized the need for accountability, particularly given Telstra’s critical role in handling emergency calls.

Despite initial claims linking a death to the outage, authorities confirmed the incident was unrelated to Triple Zero failures. However, the outage prevented some families from being notified during emergencies, compounding the distress for those affected.

AI & Tech

White-collar professionals paid to train AI that may replace them

Startups like Mercor are paying thousands of professionals to create training data for AI models, accelerating automation in their own fields. These 'human data' providers have become some of Silicon Valley's fastest-growing companies, with Mercor reaching a $10 billion valuation despite its workforce facing decreasing pay and increasing demands.

The shift from low-wage data labeling to expert-driven training marks a new phase in AI development. Where previous models relied on overseas workers tagging images or transcribing audio, current systems require domain experts to provide nuanced judgments - lawyers annotating briefs, physicists explaining concepts, or doctors detailing diagnostic processes.

This creates a paradox where professionals are essentially writing their own obsolescence manuals. As one Mercor worker described, they're creating 'golden outputs' - ideal responses that AI will later replicate - along with 'reasoning traces' that show how a human would think through the problem.

The business model thrives on contractual ambiguity. Workers are classified as independent contractors, denying them benefits or job security, while companies maintain tight control over workflow and confidentiality. Three class-action lawsuits have been filed against Mercor alleging worker misclassification.

The growth of this sector coincides with a broader trend toward contract work. By 2026, an estimated 83 million Americans will work freelance, representing over half the workforce. Unlike traditional employees, these workers disappear from official statistics when their contracts end.

AI companies face their own paradox: they need models to improve enough to demonstrate value, but not so much that they eliminate the need for ongoing human training data. This delicate balance sustains the current boom in expert data labeling services.

AI revives dead celebrities for profit as estates monetize digital likenesses

Celebrity estates are increasingly licensing deceased stars' digital likenesses for AI-generated performances and endorsements, with some estates earning hundreds of millions annually. Recent cases include AI versions of Val Kilmer in films and Whitney Houston holograms performing in Vegas. This trend raises ethical questions about posthumous consent and the commercialization of legacy.

The legal framework enabling this industry traces back to Tennessee's 1984 right of publicity law, passed after Elvis Presley's estate sued impersonators. Now, 22 states have similar laws, and the proposed federal NO FAKES Act would expand these protections nationally. California recently strengthened its laws with AB 1836, specifically protecting digital likenesses after death.

Third-party companies like Authentic Brands Group dominate the market, having acquired rights to Marilyn Monroe, Muhammad Ali, and Elvis Presley. Primary Wave Music holds stakes in Whitney Houston, Prince, and James Brown's estates. These firms treat celebrity IP as portfolios, licensing them for everything from Gucci collaborations to AI voice narration.

While some estates like Humphrey Bogart's refuse AI projects for quality concerns, others aggressively monetize. Michael Jackson's estate earned $600 million in 2026 through various licensing deals. The technology has advanced from simple holograms (Whitney Houston's 2021 Vegas show) to full AI-generated performances (James Dean's upcoming film role).

SK Hynix makes record Wall Street debut amid AI boom

SK Hynix raised $26.5 billion in the largest-ever US listing by a foreign company, with shares surging 17% to $170 on their Nasdaq debut. The South Korean memory chipmaker, a key Nvidia supplier, is capitalizing on soaring demand for AI infrastructure components like high-bandwidth memory (HBM).

The offering priced at a rare 2.9% premium to SK Hynix's Seoul-listed shares, reflecting overwhelming demand that saw orders exceed available shares by 7x. Institutional investors including sovereign wealth funds and tech-focused funds drove the appetite, according to koreaherald.com.

Proceeds will fund expansion of SK Hynix's Yongin semiconductor cluster and Cheongju packaging plant, part of South Korea's $880 billion joint investment plan with Samsung. The company has separately allocated $8.6 billion for advanced EUV lithography equipment by 2027, per regulatory filings.

The listing narrows SK Hynix's valuation gap with US rival Micron, whose shares trade at 20-40% higher P/E ratios. Nasdaq access provides liquidity for global investors who previously faced barriers to South Korea's market, notes Seoul National University's Jaewon Choi.

While Counterpoint Research forecasts memory chip margins of 75-80% through 2027, some analysts warn the cycle may peak. SK Hynix's premium pricing tests whether AI-driven enthusiasm can sustain valuations as it competes with Samsung's 38% DRAM market share.

The IPO strengthens SK Hynix's cash position for its $100 trillion won net cash target, critical as it vies with Micron and Samsung in the HBM3E race. The company supplies 90% of Nvidia's current HBM chips, according to theverge.com.

EU orders Meta to alter 'addictive' Facebook and Instagram designs

EU regulators told Meta to redesign Facebook and Instagram to reduce addictive features like infinite scroll and autoplay, or face fines up to 6% of global revenue. The preliminary findings mark Europe's most direct intervention yet in social media product design, following similar action against TikTok earlier this year.

The EU's Digital Services Act investigation into Meta began in May 2024, initially focused on child protection. Regulators have since expanded their scrutiny to platform design mechanics, mirroring their parallel case against TikTok which resulted in demands to disable infinite scrolling and modify recommendation algorithms.

Meta faces growing global pressure, with thousands of US lawsuits alleging harm to children and lobbying efforts in Congress for legal immunity. The company has implemented parental controls like nighttime access blocks and screen time caps, but regulators argue these don't address core design issues.

Europe is drafting broader 'Digital Fairness Act' legislation targeting addictive features across all platforms. The Commission awaits an expert panel's recommendations next month before finalizing Meta-specific requirements, which could set precedents for global platform regulation.

Unlike content moderation cases, this action directly challenges business models built on engagement. The 6% revenue fine threat reflects the EU's willingness to use maximum penalties under its Digital Services Act, though historical enforcement suggests final amounts may be lower.

The move aligns with global trends, including Australia's proposed social media age limits and France's push for platform design reforms. It represents a shift from debating whether young people should use social media to questioning whether platforms should access young users at all.

Biotech stocks show volatility amid sector shifts

Biotech stocks experienced mixed performance this week, with some firms like Recursion Pharmaceuticals and FB102 seeing surges driven by AI prospects and positive trial data, while others like Moderna and ImmunityBio faced steep declines. The sector’s volatility reflects broader investor rebalancing and competitive pressures, particularly from Chinese biotech firms.

The biotech sector’s recent volatility underscores a broader market shift, with investors increasingly favoring companies that demonstrate clear growth paths and operational stability. For instance, Recursion Pharmaceuticals has gained attention for its AI-driven drug discovery platform, while FB102’s promising vitiligo trial results have fueled optimism. Conversely, established players like Moderna and ImmunityBio have struggled, likely due to waning pandemic-driven demand and pipeline uncertainties. This divergence highlights the sector’s sensitivity to innovation and execution risks.

Adding to the sector’s challenges, competition from Chinese biotech firms is intensifying, prompting U.S. companies to adopt more secretive strategies to protect intellectual property. This competitive pressure is particularly acute in areas like gene therapy and biologics, where China has made significant investments.

Meanwhile, broader market trends are influencing biotech valuations. Investors are rotating capital out of high-flying tech stocks and into sectors perceived as more stable, with healthcare and biotech benefiting from this shift. However, this rebalancing has not uniformly lifted all biotech stocks, with smaller, speculative names often left behind in favor of large-cap, diversified firms.

The sector’s outlook remains tied to regulatory developments and clinical trial outcomes. Companies with strong pipelines and clear commercialization strategies are likely to attract sustained investor interest, while those facing setbacks or stiff competition may continue to struggle.
