Trump reinstates Iran blockade, imposes 20% cargo toll in Hormuz Strait

The U.S. will reinstate its naval blockade of Iranian ports and impose a 20% fee on all non-Iranian cargo transiting the Strait of Hormuz, President Trump announced Monday. The move sent oil prices surging 8% and triggered declines in global stock markets, particularly tech shares. The unilateral toll—which lacks UN legal backing—marks a dramatic escalation in tensions with Iran and threatens to disrupt a fifth of global oil trade.

The blockade reversal comes just four weeks after a June 17 ceasefire agreement that had allowed 120 million barrels of oil to transit the strait. That deal collapsed as disputes flared over control of the waterway, with Trump declaring it 'over' last week following Iranian attacks on a container ship and subsequent U.S. airstrikes on 140 targets in Iran.

Tanker traffic through the chokepoint has already plummeted 52% since Friday, with only 12 authorized crossings Sunday compared to its prewar average of 100 daily transits. The strait handled 20% of global oil and LNG trade before the conflict began in February, when prices briefly hit $120/barrel.

The White House provided no mechanism for collecting the toll, which contradicts both the UN's position that mandatory strait transit fees are illegal and Iran's claim of sovereignty over the waterway. Iranian officials called the 20% demand excessive while asserting their own right to compensation for securing the route.

Market reactions were immediate: Brent crude jumped to $82/barrel, gasoline prices rose 7 cents to $3.87/gallon nationally, and tech stocks led market declines with Nvidia dropping 3.2%. South Korea's Kospi index plunged 8.9% amid supply chain fears, its worst day since 1997.

The policy revives a concept Trump floated in June, when he suggested the U.S. could charge for 'services rendered' as Middle East security guarantor. Analysts note the U.S. previously protected Kuwaiti ships during 1980s tanker wars but never imposed fees, calling the 20% demand 'extortionate' given uncertain security outcomes.

Houthis vow retaliation after Saudi airstrikes hit Sanaa airport, threatening Yemen truce

Saudi Arabia conducted airstrikes on Sanaa International Airport to prevent an Iranian plane carrying a Houthi delegation from landing, according to Yemen's internationally recognized government. The Houthis called the attack a violation of their sovereignty and promised military retaliation, marking the first major escalation since a 2022 UN-brokered truce. The incident risks reigniting Yemen's civil war and complicating regional diplomacy amid ongoing tensions between Iran and Gulf states.

The targeted plane was returning Houthi officials from Tehran after attending the funeral of Iran's late Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. Yemen's defense ministry stated it had exhausted diplomatic channels to prevent what it called unauthorized Iranian flights into Yemeni airspace before resorting to military action.

Beyond Sanaa, the Saudi-led coalition intercepted ballistic missiles launched by Houthis toward southern Saudi Arabia hours after the airport strike. This exchange represents the first direct military confrontation between the parties since the 2022 ceasefire, which had survived despite Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping linked to the Israel-Gaza conflict.

Humanitarian operations are collateral damage: Houthis are detaining an International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) aircraft at Sanaa airport, with its crew held hostage. This comes after the failure of an ICRC-mediated prisoner exchange deal between the warring factions last week.

The UAE's 2023 withdrawal from the Saudi-led coalition created fractures in the anti-Houthi alliance, with southern separatists now controlling Aden. This fragmentation makes coordinated military responses more difficult and could prolong any renewed conflict.

UN Special Envoy Hans Grundberg warned the violence threatens Yemen's fragile stability, where 80% of the population relies on aid. The country's airspace closure — though briefly lifted — disrupts critical humanitarian flights in what remains the world's worst humanitarian crisis.

EU and partners launch $1 billion Gaza recovery fund amid ongoing conflict

The EU and over a dozen nations pledged €883.6 million ($1 billion) for Gaza's early recovery, focusing on water, sanitation, and health systems. The amount falls far short of the $71 billion the UN estimates is needed for full reconstruction over the next decade. The initiative comes as Israel maintains control over 70% of Gaza despite a fragile ceasefire.

The 'Team Gaza Initiative' marks the first coordinated international effort to address Gaza's infrastructure collapse since the 2023 war. Unlike past reconstruction plans, this focuses on immediate 'early recovery' needs rather than long-term rebuilding, reflecting donor concerns about ongoing instability. The EU's Dubravka Šuica emphasized restoring basic governance functions alongside infrastructure, signaling a push to strengthen the Palestinian Authority's role.

Israel's withholding of Palestinian tax revenues—critical for paying public sector salaries—emerged as a key obstacle during the Brussels conference. The EU explicitly called for their release, highlighting how political disputes continue to hamper recovery efforts even as humanitarian needs escalate. Over 90% of Gaza's population remains displaced, with most living in damaged buildings or tents.

Jared Kushner's participation via videolink suggests continued U.S. engagement despite the Trump administration's exit from direct mediation. His involvement drew attention given his family's close ties to Netanyahu and his controversial 2020 peace plan, which proposed $50 billion in regional investments but was rejected by Palestinians.

The funding breakdown reveals a strategic approach: 30% comes from EU institutions (€257M), with the rest split among 14 nations and the World Bank. This distribution avoids over-reliance on any single donor while incorporating major players like Germany and Japan. Notably absent are Gulf states, traditionally key funders of Palestinian projects.

The initiative faces operational hurdles beyond funding shortages. Israel's 'buffer zone' policy has rendered 70% of Gaza inaccessible for reconstruction, while Hamas and PA governance disputes persist. The EU's conditional phrasing—'when conditions allow'—acknowledges these barriers may delay implementation indefinitely.

President Donald Trump recommended Darline Graham Nordone, sister of the late Sen. Lindsey Graham, to fill his Senate seat through the rest of the year. Graham died unexpectedly on July 11 from an aortic dissection due to arteriosclerotic cardiovascular disease. Under South Carolina law, Gov. Henry McMaster will appoint an interim replacement before a special election in August.

Implications

The appointment will temporarily fill a key Senate seat as Republicans hold a narrow majority. The interim senator will serve until January 2027, when the winner of the November general election is sworn in. The special primary election on August 11 will determine the Republican candidate to face Democrat Annie Andrews in November.

How different sides see it

Republicans: President Trump and Sen. Tim Scott argue that appointing Graham's sister would be a fitting tribute to the late senator, with Scott stating she 'understands Lindsey's love for family, our state, and our country.'

Democrats: Democrats, led by candidate Dr. Annie Andrews, are preparing for the November general election, with the Cook Political Report labeling the race as 'Solid R,' indicating a strong Republican advantage.

Potential Republican candidates: Several Republican representatives, including Nancy Mace, Ralph Norman, and William Timmons, have expressed interest in running for the seat in the special primary, while Lt. Gov. Pamela Evette is also considered a top contender for the interim appointment.

Judge Rebukes Trump’s IRS Lawsuit as Self-Dealing

A federal judge in Florida ruled that President Donald Trump filed a $10 billion lawsuit against the IRS 'for an improper purpose' to legitimize a controversial settlement with the DOJ. The settlement, which included protections for Trump and his family from audits, was nullified by the judge, who also referred Trump's lawyer to the Florida bar for potential disciplinary action. The DOJ's $1.8 billion 'lawfare' fund, created under the settlement, was abandoned after congressional backlash.

Implications

The ruling prevents Trump and the DOJ from using the settlement in future legal proceedings and removes audit protections for Trump and his family regarding pre-settlement tax returns. The DOJ is unlikely to revive the $1.8 billion fund, which faced legal and political challenges. The judge's referral of Trump's lawyer to disciplinary bodies could impact his legal standing and future cases.

How different sides see it

Democrats: Democrats argue the ruling upholds the rule of law and prevents the executive branch from abusing its power for personal gain. They highlight the judge's finding that the lawsuit was filed for an improper purpose and praise the decision to refer Trump's lawyer for disciplinary review.

Republicans: Republicans, including Trump's legal team, contend that the lawsuit was necessary to hold the IRS accountable for allegedly leaking confidential information to the media. They argue the judge's ruling undermines efforts to protect taxpayers from politically motivated actions by government agencies.

Former Judges: The 35 former judges who filed the brief argue the settlement was an improper exercise of executive power and applaud the court's decision to nullify it, calling it a victory for judicial integrity and the rule of law.

USPTO rejects 77% of AI patent applications under Section 101

The U.S. Patent Office is rejecting 77% of AI-related patent applications under Section 101's abstract idea prohibition, nearing pre-2019 levels. This creates significant hurdles for protecting AI innovations despite government promotion of the technology.

The spike follows a period of relative stability from 2021-2023 when Section 101 rejections hovered around 16-17%. The current 23% overall rejection rate approaches the 25% peak seen before the 2019 Patent Eligibility Guidance (PEG) that temporarily eased restrictions.

Examiners in AI-focused Group 2120 face internal pressures to issue Section 101 rejections, including quality review penalties for missed rejections and supervisory pressure to maintain high rejection rates. This systemic bias persists despite MPEP 2106.04(a)(1)'s example approving neural network training methods.

The trend contradicts the USPTO's public support for AI development. Former Director David Kappos warned in 2019 that restrictive Section 101 interpretations undermine investment in emerging technologies like AI.

Patent applicants face an 'art unit lottery' - applications in Group 3690 face 90% rejection rates, while others see far lower hurdles. Drafters must now strategically structure applications to avoid high-rejection art units.

The USPTO is reportedly developing new Section 101 guidance, raising concerns it may further restrict protections rather than clarify eligibility for AI innovations as the 2019 PEG attempted.

Heavy AI adopters grow workforces 10.2%, with entry-level hiring up 12%

Companies investing heavily in AI grew headcount by 10.2% over two years post-adoption, with entry-level roles expanding 12%, according to a Ramp Economics Lab study of 21,599 firms. The findings counter widespread fears of AI-driven job losses, showing the technology's adoption correlates with hiring — particularly in tech-adjacent sectors.

The study uniquely links corporate card spending on AI tools to workforce data, revealing a $34/month per employee spending threshold where hiring effects become significant. High-intensity adopters — typically VC-backed tech firms — saw compounding gains starting 6-12 months after implementation, suggesting an organizational learning curve precedes productivity benefits.

Small businesses showed asymmetric impacts: while less likely to adopt AI, those that did spent more intensively and saw higher marginal gains. The authors speculate this reflects AI enabling capabilities previously requiring specialized teams, though adoption barriers remain for smaller firms without venture funding or technical networks.

Sector disparities were stark — information firms (media, software, internet) captured most gains, while other industries showed neutral effects. Engineering roles expanded alongside entry-level positions, contradicting assumptions that AI would disproportionately automate junior or technical jobs first.

The paper cautions against extrapolating its findings to all firms, noting adopters were already faster-growing before AI investment. Researchers emphasize these are early results in a rapidly evolving landscape, with future updates planned as cheaper tools democratize access.

BRAIN Biotech secures €9M revolving credit line

BRAIN Biotech AG signed a €9 million revolving credit facility with MP Beteiligungs-GmbH, with a variable interest rate of Euribor plus 233 basis points through December 2028. The funds will support general corporate financing and debt servicing. This provides liquidity flexibility for the German biotech firm during a period of rising borrowing costs.

The credit line's interest rate structure reflects current market conditions, with the 233 basis point spread over Euribor indicating lender confidence in BRAIN's creditworthiness. For context, the 6-month Euribor stood at 3.8% as of July 2026, making the effective rate approximately 6.13% initially.

MP Beteiligungs-GmbH, the lender, is a German investment company specializing in mid-cap financing. Their involvement suggests BRAIN met stringent due diligence requirements, as MPBG typically focuses on stable companies with clear growth trajectories in tech and life sciences.

Revolving credit facilities differ from term loans by allowing borrowers to draw, repay, and redraw funds as needed. This structure is particularly valuable for biotech firms like BRAIN that face irregular cash flows due to R&D cycles and milestone-based revenue.

The announcement follows BRAIN's Q2 2026 earnings report showing €14.2M in cash reserves, indicating this credit line serves as a strategic buffer rather than emergency funding. The company recently expanded its bioindustrial enzyme production capacity, requiring working capital for scaled operations.
