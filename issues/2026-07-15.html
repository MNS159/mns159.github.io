France approves assisted dying law after years of debate

France's National Assembly voted 291-241 to approve a law allowing assisted dying for adults with incurable, terminal illnesses causing unbearable suffering. The decision follows years of contentious debate and three rejections by the Senate. France could join several other European countries that have decriminalized some form of assisted dying.

The law requires patients to administer the lethal substance themselves, though doctors or nurses can assist if the patient is unable to do so. Critics argue the two-day reflection period is too short and question whether patients with impaired judgement can give informed consent. Prime Minister Sébastien Lecornu has referred these aspects to France's Constitutional Council for review. The issue has faced strong opposition from the Catholic Church and parts of the medical profession, though polls show most French citizens support it. France's approval contrasts with the UK, where a similar bill stalled earlier this year and is set to return to Parliament in September. The Netherlands and Belgium pioneered physician-assisted dying in 2002, followed by several other European countries and Switzerland, which allows assisted suicide if done unselfishly.

Congressional scrutiny of ICE's use of force and reliance on traffic stops is likely to increase.

Trump administration: President Trump and ICE officials argue that traffic stops are a crucial enforcement tool for immigration control and public safety. They emphasize the need for officers to remain vigilant but act judiciously.

Democrats and immigration advocates: Critics, including Democratic lawmakers and advocacy groups, argue that ICE's use of traffic stops has led to unnecessary fatalities and violates due process. They call for stricter oversight and accountability measures.

Affected communities: Protesters and families of the deceased dispute ICE's accounts of the incidents, alleging excessive force and demanding transparency, including the release of body camera footage and independent investigations.

House passes bill to make daylight saving time permanent

The U.S. House of Representatives passed the Sunshine Protection Act on a 308-117 vote, which would establish permanent daylight saving time nationwide. The bill now moves to the Senate, where its prospects are uncertain due to some Republican opposition. Nineteen states have passed similar legislation but cannot implement it without federal approval.

Implications

If enacted, permanent daylight saving time would eliminate biannual clock changes, potentially reducing associated health risks like heart attacks and traffic accidents linked to sleep disruption. However, sleep experts warn permanent daylight time may misalign with natural circadian rhythms. The change would also create later sunrises in winter months across most of the U.S.

How different sides see it

Supporters (including President Trump): Advocates argue permanent DST will save hundreds of millions in administrative costs and eliminate public health risks associated with clock changes, while providing more evening daylight year-round for economic and recreational activities.

Opponents (including some Senate Republicans): Critics contend permanent DST would create dangerous morning darkness for schoolchildren and workers, with Sen. Cotton calling it harmful. Sleep medicine experts prefer permanent standard time for better circadian alignment.

States with existing exemptions: Arizona and Hawaii (on permanent standard time) would remain unaffected. The 19 states that passed permanent DST laws await federal approval to implement their policies.

Suno AI music generator accused of scraping millions of songs without permission

Hacked files reveal Suno trained its AI models by scraping millions of songs from YouTube Music, Deezer, and Genius without permission, according to 404 Media. The data supports ongoing lawsuits alleging copyright infringement, including one by the RIAA that claims Suno circumvented YouTube's protections. This comes as Suno faces scrutiny over its opaque training data practices and a separate security breach exposing customer information.

The leaked files include source code showing Suno's systematic scraping of protected platforms, with instructions to pull audio from YouTube Music, Deezer, and others. One dataset contained over 2 million YouTube Music clips, while others aggregated hundreds of thousands of hours of audio from multiple sources. The code also suggests Suno targeted acapella tracks specifically, likely to isolate vocal data for AI training.

Suno has defended its practices by claiming fair use of publicly available internet content, a stance being challenged in court. The RIAA's amended lawsuit alleges Suno used 'stream ripping' tools to bypass YouTube's copyright protections, which could violate the Digital Millennium Copyright Act if proven. Legal experts note this case may set precedents for how AI companies source training data.

Third-party involvement appears in the leaks, with Bright Data allegedly hired to scrape YouTube. Suno also attempted to download 1 million hours of podcasts via PodcastIndex, indicating a broad approach to sourcing diverse audio content. The scale suggests Suno prioritized data quantity over licensing clarity during its rapid development phase.

Separately, the November 2025 hack exposed customer emails, phone numbers, and partial payment details, though Suno claims no full credit card numbers were compromised. The company's decision not to notify users about the breach has drawn criticism, as several confirmed they were unaware their data had been accessed until contacted by journalists.

Microsoft patches record 622 vulnerabilities using AI-driven tools

Microsoft released patches for a record 622 vulnerabilities in its July 2026 Patch Tuesday update, including two exploited zero-days in Active Directory Federation Services and SharePoint Server. The surge in patched vulnerabilities comes as Microsoft accelerates bug discovery through AI-driven tools like MDASH, marking a shift in enterprise software security.

The MDASH AI system uses multi-model agentic scanning to rapidly surface bugs across Microsoft's codebase, including decades-old Windows code where vulnerabilities may have lain dormant. Microsoft executives note this AI-assisted discovery is fundamentally changing their approach to vulnerability management, moving from reactive fixes to integrating bug finding directly into the development process.

Among the critical flaws patched, CVE-2026-56155 allows local privilege escalation in Active Directory Federation Services, while CVE-2026-56164 enables unauthenticated privilege escalation attacks on SharePoint Server. CISA has urged organizations running SharePoint to deploy additional hardening measures beyond patching.

The July update also addresses CVE-2026-50661, a BitLocker security feature bypass that was publicly disclosed before patching. Microsoft attributes the ballooning patch volumes to AI tools used both internally and by external researchers, warning that organizations must accelerate deployment timelines to close the widening window of exploitability.

China’s Economy Grows at Slowest Pace in Years

China’s economy grew by 4.3% in the second quarter of 2026, marking its slowest pace in three years and falling short of economists’ expectations. This slowdown reflects persistent challenges in the property market, weak domestic demand, and uneven recovery in consumer spending, despite a surge in exports.

The 4.3% growth rate represents the lowest quarterly expansion since China emerged from strict Covid-19 restrictions in late 2022. The property market, a key driver of China’s economy, continues to slump, with new home prices contracting in June, albeit at a slower pace. Retail sales, though improving slightly, remain weak, signaling tepid consumer confidence.

China’s export sector, however, remains robust, buoyed by global demand for semiconductors and electric vehicles, which saw monthly car exports top one million for the first time. This divergence highlights an imbalance between strong supply in manufacturing and weak domestic demand.

Analysts note that the slowdown may partly reflect Beijing’s decision to lower its annual growth target to 4.5%-5% in March, acknowledging pre-existing economic weakness rather than a sudden deterioration. The government has emphasized external uncertainties, including the impact of geopolitical conflicts and fluctuating oil prices, as additional pressures on growth.

Insilico and Bora Pharmaceuticals forge $2.5B AI drug development deal

AI drug discovery firm Insilico Medicine partnered with Taiwan-based CDMO Bora Pharmaceuticals in a deal potentially worth $2.5 billion to integrate AI across drug development and manufacturing. The collaboration aims to accelerate preclinical candidate generation from 2.5-4 years to 12-18 months while optimizing Bora's production workflows. This marks a key expansion of AI's role beyond discovery into full-scale pharmaceutical operations.

Bora has aggressively expanded its US footprint through acquisitions, including $210 million for Upsher-Smith in 2024 and $127 million for MacroGenics' CDMO operations recently. The Insilico deal represents its first major move into AI-enhanced manufacturing.

Insilico's Pharma.AI platform has demonstrated rapid output, generating 31 preclinical candidates since 2021 with 13 reaching IND stage. The partnership will test whether similar efficiency gains can be achieved in manufacturing automation and supply chain logistics.

Unlike Insilico's previous discovery-focused deals with Takeda ($660M potential) and SK Biosciences ($2.5B potential), this collaboration specifically targets 'fill-finish' production capabilities at Bora's newly acquired Baltimore plant.

The deal reflects a broader industry trend where CDMOs like Bora are transitioning from service providers to innovation hubs, with AI as a core competitive differentiator in biologics manufacturing.

Insilico now has partnerships spanning the drug development lifecycle - from Lilly's early-stage target discovery to Bora's commercial-scale production - creating a vertically integrated AI pipeline unprecedented in biotech.
