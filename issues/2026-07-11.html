Trump and Iran exchange threats amid escalating tensions over Strait of Hormuz

Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei vowed revenge for his father's assassination while President Trump threatened 'complete decimation' of Iran if any attack targets him. Both nations continue trading threats over control of the Strait of Hormuz, even as Qatari and Omani mediators attempt to de-escalate tensions through diplomatic channels.

The crisis stems from the February 28 assassination of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, which Iran blames on the U.S. and Israel. His successor son Mojtaba has framed revenge as state policy, with hardliners dominating public rhetoric while negotiators like Foreign Minister Araghchi pursue talks in Oman.

Trump's threat of 'complete decimation' references a pre-authorized one-year military campaign against Iran, suggesting contingency plans are already in place. This comes after both sides exchanged fire in the Strait of Hormuz earlier this week, with Iran insisting on sole management of the critical waterway.

Behind the scenes, Qatari mediators visited Tehran while Iranian officials reportedly told Washington that hardline factions were responsible for recent attacks on shipping. The U.S. appears to be using this narrative to keep diplomacy alive while maintaining military pressure.

Iran established a Persian Gulf Strait Authority to control transit, but the International Maritime Organization condemned this move. The strait normally handles 20% of global oil shipments, with traffic currently at half of pre-war levels despite Iran's claims of cooperation.

The conflict risks disrupting global energy markets, as the strait is the only sea passage for Gulf oil exports. Both sides face domestic pressure - Trump from election-year hawks, Khamenei from hardliners who view negotiations as treason.

South Africa World Cup midfielder Jayden Adams dies at 25

South African midfielder Jayden Adams, who played in all three of his country's group matches at the 2026 World Cup, has died at age 25. Authorities found his body in a Cape Town suburb, with the cause of death under investigation. Adams was a rising star who helped South Africa reach the World Cup knockout stages for the first time.

Adams' death comes just 16 days after his World Cup appearance, where he started against the Czech Republic despite learning of his grandmother's death hours before kickoff. His performance under emotional duress exemplified his professionalism.

The midfielder had recently won domestic and continental titles with Mamelodi Sundowns after transferring from Stellenbosch FC in 2025. His career trajectory saw rapid growth from 2022 international debut to World Cup starter.

South Africa's historic World Cup run, where they became the first African team to advance from a group containing two European sides (Czech Republic and Ukraine), featured Adams in every match. His midfield work rate was crucial to their tactical setup.

Police have not disclosed potential causes of death, but the sudden nature has drawn comparisons to other young athlete tragedies. The sports minister specifically requested media restraint regarding speculation.

Adams' death marks the second high-profile loss for South African football in recent years, following the 2022 passing of former Orlando Pirates goalkeeper Senzo Meyiwa. Both cases involved sudden, unexplained deaths of national team players.

Wildfire in southern Spain kills at least 12, including British nationals

A fast-moving wildfire in Spain's Almería province has killed at least 12 people, including four British nationals, with 23 still missing. The blaze, fueled by 40°C heat and strong winds, has burned 6,600 hectares and marks one of Spain's deadliest wildfires in decades.

The fire's rapid spread was exacerbated by a prolonged heatwave affecting southern Europe, with temperatures consistently exceeding 40°C (104°F). This follows similar wildfires in France and Portugal, where thousands have been evacuated.

Authorities suspect a fallen power line may have ignited the blaze, though local utility companies dispute this claim. The fire's intensity prevented direct firefighting efforts until weather conditions improved on Saturday.

Many victims appear to be foreign nationals, with Belgian officials also attempting to locate missing citizens who own second homes in the area. The British Foreign Office has not yet released identities of the deceased.

Climate scientists note that Europe is warming twice as fast as the global average, leading to more frequent and intense wildfires. The current heatwave follows record-breaking temperatures across the continent in recent years.

This wildfire ranks among Spain's deadliest since 1984, when 20 died in the Canary Islands, and 1979, when 21 perished near Lloret de Mar. French authorities have separately arrested 32 suspects for allegedly starting fires in the Pyrenees region.

15 Indian tourists killed in Vietnam speedboat capsizing

A speedboat carrying 32 Indian tourists capsized near Hon May Rut Ngoai island in Vietnam, killing 15 people. Rough sea conditions may have caused the accident, with 21 survivors rescued by nearby vessels. The incident highlights safety concerns for Vietnam's growing tourism industry.

The accident occurred in the An Thoi Archipelago, a popular destination for island-hopping tours near Phu Quoc, Vietnam's largest island. This marks the second fatal maritime incident in Vietnam this week, following a separate boat capsizing in Quang Ninh province that killed two people.

Vietnam's maritime safety record has come under scrutiny, with at least three major tourist boat accidents reported since 2025. The country received over 18 million international tourists in 2025, with water-based activities becoming increasingly popular.

Indian tourists represent a growing market for Vietnam, with arrivals increasing by 42% year-on-year in 2025. The Indian embassy has activated emergency protocols, including publishing a passenger manifest and establishing dedicated helplines for affected families.

Local authorities are investigating whether the vessel was operating within safety regulations. Vietnam's Prime Minister Pham Minh Chinh has ordered urgent inspections of all tourist watercraft following the back-to-back accidents.

Gordie Howe Bridge to open July 27 after US-Canada revenue deal

The Gordie Howe International Bridge between Detroit and Windsor will open July 27 after Canada and the U.S. agreed to split toll revenues and establish a joint economic fund. The resolution follows a delay triggered by Trump administration demands to renegotiate terms of the $4.7 billion project.

Under the revised agreement, the U.S. will receive up to 50% of toll profits after operational expenses, directed to a 15-year regional development fund. This marks a shift from the original 2012 deal where Canada would recoup all construction costs before revenue sharing. The Windsor-Detroit Bridge Authority must now consult U.S. officials on toll changes exceeding 10% or dropping below regional averages.

The bridge’s opening was delayed from June after President Trump threatened to block it unless Canada conceded to unspecified trade demands. Negotiations were led by U.S. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick and Ambassador Pete Hoekstra, with Trump claiming victory by tweeting 'MUCH BETTER DEAL for America' on Saturday.

A University of Windsor study projects the bridge will save trucks $2.3 billion over 30 years by cutting crossing times. The six-lane span is expected to relieve congestion on the nearby Ambassador Bridge, which handles 25% of all U.S.-Canada trade by road.

The deal reportedly includes provisions barring Chinese-made EVs from entering the U.S. via the bridge, aligning with America’s 100% tariff on such imports. Canada currently allows 49,000 Chinese EVs annually under a 6.1% tariff agreement with China.

Bipartisan US housing bill becomes law without Trump's signature

The 21st Century Road to Housing Act became law without President Trump's signature after he refused to sign it in protest over Congress's failure to pass voter ID legislation. The bill, which passed both chambers of Congress in June, includes over 40 provisions aimed at reducing housing costs and increasing supply. The median price of existing homes reached a record $440,660 in June, according to the National Association of Realtors.

Implications

The law's enactment without a veto signals a rare bipartisan achievement on housing policy, which could impact affordability and supply in the coming years. The political fallout may influence midterm elections, as housing costs are a key voter concern. Trump's refusal to sign highlights ongoing tensions between his priorities and congressional action.

How different sides see it

Democrats: Democrats argue the housing bill addresses urgent affordability crises and criticize Trump for prioritizing voter ID laws over housing relief. House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries stated Republicans 'would rather make it harder to vote than easier to afford a home.'

Republicans: Republicans, including Trump, emphasize the need for voter ID laws as a precondition for supporting other legislation. Trump called the SAVE Act essential to election integrity, though GOP leaders acknowledge insufficient Senate support to pass it.

The Trump administration has subpoenaed several New York Times journalists, requiring them to testify before a federal grand jury regarding their reporting on alleged security concerns involving President Trump's new Air Force One plane. The journalists reported that the plane, gifted by Qatar, lacked advanced security features, prompting the Secret Service to advise Trump to switch planes during a NATO summit. The Justice Department stated it is investigating illegal leaks of classified information, emphasizing that reporters are not the targets.

Implications

The subpoenas could escalate tensions between the Trump administration and the press, raising concerns about press freedom and the ability of journalists to report on government operations without fear of legal repercussions. This move may also deter future whistleblowers from coming forward with sensitive information, potentially limiting public access to critical government insights. The case could set a precedent for how classified information leaks are handled in relation to journalists.

How different sides see it

Democrats: Democrats argue that the subpoenas represent an abuse of power by the Trump administration, aimed at intimidating journalists and suppressing press freedom. They emphasize the First Amendment’s protection of the press and warn that such actions could undermine democratic accountability.

Republicans: Republicans defend the Justice Department’s actions, stating that the investigation is focused on identifying and prosecuting those who illegally leaked classified information. They argue that national security must be prioritized and that the subpoenas are a legitimate step to protect sensitive government information.

The New York Times: The New York Times asserts that the subpoenas are an attempt to intimidate journalists and prevent the public from accessing important information about government operations. The paper’s legal counsel, David McCraw, described the move as a 'brazen act' aimed at undermining the press’s role in holding the government accountable.

U.S. District Judge Timothy Kelly, a Trump appointee, on Friday granted the Justice Department’s motion to dismiss the seditious conspiracy convictions of four Proud Boys members: Ethan Nordean, Joseph Biggs, Zachary Rehl, and Dominic Pezzola. The decision follows a May ruling by a federal appeals court that vacated the convictions and returned the case to the district court. Kelly emphasized that the dismissal was a procedural necessity, not an endorsement of the administration’s decision.

Implications

The dismissal permanently bars the federal government from bringing the same charges against the four defendants again. It also erases some of the most significant convictions from the January 6 investigation, which had been one of the largest federal probes in U.S. history. The ruling underscores the executive branch’s broad authority over criminal charging decisions, a principle long upheld by U.S. courts.

How different sides see it

Democrats: Democrats argue that the dismissal undermines accountability for the January 6 Capitol attack, which they describe as an assault on democracy. They criticize the Trump administration’s decision to abandon the prosecution as a political maneuver that prioritizes loyalty over justice.

Republicans: Republicans contend that the dismissals are a necessary course correction, arguing that the January 6 prosecutions were overzealous and politically motivated. They support President Trump’s view that the prosecutions unfairly targeted his supporters and that clemency is justified.

AI wealth boom sparks calls for economic safeguards as inequality grows

AI startups have created 498 unicorns worth $2.7 trillion since 2023, minting dozens of new billionaires while public trust in AI plummets to 16%. Economists warn the US lacks concrete plans to address potential mass job displacement or redistribute AI-generated wealth, unlike the prepared 'break the glass' financial crisis response of 2008.

The AI wealth surge differs from previous tech booms in its concentration among private companies - 73% of AI unicorns remain privately held, enabling founders to access liquidity through secondary markets while avoiding public scrutiny. This contrasts with the dot-com era's IPO frenzy, delaying wealth redistribution mechanisms like capital gains taxes.

Policy proposals split between tech executives' vague concepts (OpenAI's sovereign wealth fund idea lacks funding specifics) and Washington's incremental approaches (Gina Raimondo's retraining programs). MIT researchers note this gap mirrors pre-2008 financial crisis planning failures, when only the Treasury's detailed 'Break the Glass Plan' proved actionable during emergency conditions.

Emerging secondary markets complicate wealth tracking, as seen when Scale AI's co-founder Lucy Guo purchased a $30M mansion using shares sold privately. Unlike public company disclosures, these transactions occur without transparency about valuation methods or tax obligations.

The Center for Shared AI Prosperity argues current retraining proposals underestimate disruption speed - AI could eliminate entire job categories faster than community colleges can design new certificates. Their emergency plan templates include radical ideas like universal capital accounts, modeled after Alaska's oil dividend system but funded by AI productivity gains.

Public distrust (63% say AI advances too quickly) coincides with unprecedented valuation growth - Anthropic's potential $170B valuation would triple its March worth. This tension mirrors pre-Great Depression wealth gaps, when technological productivity gains outpaced worker compensation and policy responses.

AI demand drives memory shortages, forcing Apple to raise prices

Apple has raised prices on select Mac and iPad models by 20% due to soaring memory costs, CEO Tim Cook called the situation a "100-year flood." AI servers are competing with consumer devices for limited memory supplies, with shortages expected to persist through 2027. Micron's HBM supply is sold out through 2026, signaling AI's pressure on memory markets.

AI data centers are consuming an unprecedented share of global memory production, particularly high-bandwidth memory (HBM) and DRAM. Micron, a key supplier, has reported that it can only fulfill 50-66% of customer demand for these components, despite record profits. This scarcity has forced memory manufacturers to prioritize higher-margin AI products over the commodity DRAM used in consumer devices like laptops and smartphones.

Apple, as the world’s largest buyer of memory chips, is particularly vulnerable to these market dynamics. The company's recent price hikes reflect the global competition for memory between AI infrastructure and consumer electronics. SK Hynix predicts the worst memory shortage will hit in 2027, with demand outpacing supply well into the next decade.

While memory manufacturers like Micron, Samsung, and SK Hynix are investing billions to expand capacity, production leans toward AI-focused memory. This leaves consumer electronics companies with fewer options, forcing them to either absorb higher costs or pass them on to customers. PC manufacturers, which compete on price, face a particularly tough choice, potentially extending device replacement cycles.

The situation illustrates the broader economic ripple effects of the AI boom. While GPU shortages have dominated headlines, memory has emerged as a critical bottleneck in the semiconductor industry. This trend, dubbed "AI-flation," is reshaping market dynamics, with manufacturers prioritizing AI infrastructure over consumer electronics. Apple's pricing power may help it weather the storm, but the broader industry faces sustained challenges.

NVIDIA and Lilly launch $1B AI drug discovery lab

NVIDIA and Eli Lilly announced a $1 billion joint AI lab to accelerate drug discovery, combining pharmaceutical expertise with AI computing power. The collaboration aims to model biological systems digitally before physical testing, potentially transforming the industry's R&D timeline.

The lab will use NVIDIA's BioNeMo platform to create a continuous learning system, linking physical wet labs with computational dry labs in a 24/7 feedback loop. This 'scientist-in-the-loop' framework allows real-time data sharing between experiments and AI models, a first for pharmaceutical research at this scale.

Digital twin technology from NVIDIA Omniverse will simulate manufacturing lines and supply chains. This approach mirrors NVIDIA's work in other industries but applies it to drug production bottlenecks, addressing recent pharmaceutical supply chain vulnerabilities exposed during global health crises.

Lilly's existing AI supercomputer - the most powerful in pharma - will train biomedical foundation models. Unlike earlier AI drug discovery efforts focused on narrow targets, this collaboration aims to build frontier models capable of multi-dimensional biological simulations.

The initiative expands beyond discovery into clinical development and manufacturing. Agentic AI and robotics will optimize production of high-demand medications like Lilly's obesity and Alzheimer's drugs, where scaling production has been challenging.
